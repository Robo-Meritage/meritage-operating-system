# Operating Principles

> The rules that govern how Meritage runs — decisions, communication, and work.
>
> ---
>
> ## Why Principles Matter
>
> Principles are not aspirations. They are the actual operating rules we hold ourselves to when the situation is ambiguous, when it's inconvenient, or when no one is watching.
>
> Every company has principles. Most are written on a wall and ignored. Ours are meant to be used — in hiring decisions, in client negotiations, in disagreements, and in daily work.
>
> When a principle is violated, name it. When a principle is wrong, change it.
>
> ---
>
> ## The Principles
>
> ### 1. Operators first
>
> We are operators who advise, not advisors who theorize. Every recommendation we make has to be something we'd implement ourselves. If we wouldn't stake our own capital or time on it, we don't recommend it to a client.
>
> This applies internally too. We don't build processes we wouldn't want to work inside of.
>
> ---
>
> ### 2. Transparency is cheaper than damage control
>
> Telling the truth is always cheaper than managing the fallout from not telling it. This means:
>
> - Telling clients when their business isn't ready to sell
> - - Telling teammates when their work isn't good enough
>   - - Telling ourselves when a plan isn't working
>    
>     - We don't soften feedback to the point where it loses meaning. We deliver it with respect, but we deliver it.
>    
>     - ---
>
> ### 3. Simple over clever
>
> Complexity is a liability. It's harder to execute, harder to explain, and harder to fix. When given a choice between a simple solution and a sophisticated one, we choose simple — unless the problem genuinely requires sophistication.
>
> This applies to deal structures, documents, systems, and communication.
>
> ---
>
> ### 4. Written over verbal
>
> If it's not written down, it didn't happen. Every decision, commitment, and agreement that matters should be documented.
>
> This protects everyone: clients, teammates, and the business. It also forces clarity — vague ideas become precise when you try to write them down.
>
> MOS is the mechanism. Use it.
>
> ---
>
> ### 5. Relationships over transactions
>
> Every transaction we close is the beginning of a relationship, not the end of one. We treat former clients as future referral sources, advisors, and partners.
>
> Short-term thinking loses long-term value. A client who felt taken care of is worth ten times the fee we earned from them.
>
> ---
>
> ### 6. Ownership without excuses
>
> We own outcomes, not just effort. "I tried" is not a result. When something goes wrong, we focus first on fixing it, then on understanding why, then on preventing it.
>
> This applies from the top down. Leadership cannot demand accountability from others while making excuses for themselves.
>
> ---
>
> ### 7. Speed is a competitive advantage
>
> In our market, responsiveness is rare. Responding the same day when competitors respond in a week is a differentiator. Moving quickly through due diligence when buyers expect delays earns trust.
>
> Speed doesn't mean carelessness. It means urgency — treating other people's time and timelines as seriously as our own.
>
> ---
>
> ### 8. Fewer things, done completely
>
> We'd rather finish three things than start seven. Incomplete work creates drag: mental overhead, client confusion, and organizational complexity.
>
> When prioritization is required, we cut mercilessly. A shorter list, executed completely, is always better than a longer list with nothing finished.
>
> ---
>
> ### 9. Learn from every deal
>
> Every transaction teaches us something. What did we underestimate? What should we have caught in due diligence? Where did the timeline break down?
>
> We capture those lessons in MOS so we don't repeat the same mistakes. A firm that doesn't learn from its deals is just a broker. We are not a broker.
>
> ---
>
> ### 10. Protect the brand by being selective
>
> The Meritage name is the most valuable thing we have. We protect it by being selective: with clients we take on, buyers we introduce, and claims we make.
>
> We don't take every engagement. We don't promise results we can't control. We don't cut corners when it's inconvenient not to.
>
> ---
>
> ## Applying the Principles
>
> Principles are most useful when there's a conflict — when two reasonable people disagree, or when a shortcut is tempting.
>
> When you're unsure what to do, ask:
> - Which principle is most relevant here?
> - - What would this principle tell us to do?
>   - - Am I violating a principle to make something easier?
>    
>     - If the answer to the last question is yes, that's a signal to stop.
>    
>     - ---
>
> ## Changing the Principles
>
> These principles are not sacred. They can and should be updated when evidence suggests they're wrong, incomplete, or no longer relevant.
>
> To propose a change:
> 1. Describe the current principle
> 2. 2. Describe the problem with it
>    3. 3. Propose the revision
>       4. 4. Submit it as an ADR in `docs/decisions/`
>          5. 5. Get sign-off from at least one other senior person
