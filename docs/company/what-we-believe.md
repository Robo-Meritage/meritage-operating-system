# What We Believe

> The beliefs that shape how Meritage thinks, advises, and operates. Not aspirations — convictions.
>
> ---
>
> ## Why Beliefs, Not Values
>
> Values are what you aspire to. Beliefs are what you act on.
>
> Most companies publish values that are too broad to guide decisions: "integrity," "excellence," "teamwork." Those words are true of every company that prints them and used by almost none.
>
> Beliefs are different. A belief is a statement you'd defend in an argument. It has implications. It rules things out. If you can believe the opposite without consequence, it's not a belief — it's decoration.
>
> The beliefs below shape how we advise clients, evaluate deals, hire people, and run the business. They are the lens.
>
> ---
>
> ## Our Beliefs
>
> ---
>
> ### Great businesses are built through execution, not luck.
>
> A founder who attributes their success entirely to timing or market conditions is selling themselves short — and usually wrong. The businesses that compound value over time do so because someone made hard decisions consistently and followed through.
>
> This belief means we take execution seriously. We don't accept "the market is bad" as an explanation for declining performance without examining what operational decisions contributed. And we hold ourselves to the same standard.
>
> ---
>
> ### Relationships compound.
>
> A single transaction is worth one fee. A trusted relationship is worth a decade of referrals, repeat business, and institutional knowledge that makes every subsequent engagement easier and better.
>
> We optimize for the relationship, not the transaction. This sometimes means telling a client something they don't want to hear, walking away from a deal that isn't right, or spending more time on post-close service than the economics justify. We do it anyway because we believe it compounds.
>
> ---
>
> ### Operations create enterprise value.
>
> The multiple a business sells for is directly correlated to how well it runs. Clean financials, documented processes, reliable revenue, and a management team that doesn't depend on the founder — each of these increases valuation in a measurable way.
>
> This belief is why Robosky Consulting exists. Operational improvement before a sale isn't just nice to have. It's value creation. We help clients understand this distinction early.
>
> ---
>
> ### We optimize for long-term trust over short-term revenue.
>
> The temptation in advisory is to take every engagement, push every deal toward close, and maximize fees in the near term. We've seen what that looks like. Advisors who operate that way get deals done in the short term and lose reputations in the long term.
>
> We are selective about clients. We walk away from deals that aren't right. We tell sellers when their expectations aren't realistic. This costs short-term revenue. It builds a business worth having.
>
> ---
>
> ### Every recommendation should be supported by evidence.
>
> Opinion is easy. Evidence is harder. We expect our advice to be grounded in data, precedent, or direct experience — not intuition alone.
>
> This doesn't mean we don't make judgment calls. It means we know the difference between a judgment call and an evidence-based recommendation, and we're honest about which one we're making.
>
> ---
>
> ### We challenge assumptions before making decisions.
>
> Most bad decisions follow from unchallenged assumptions. "The buyer is serious." "The seller is motivated." "The EBITDA is accurate." "This will close on time."
>
> Our job is to ask the questions that reveal which assumptions are solid and which are wishful. We do this for clients. We do this for ourselves. A team that can't challenge its own assumptions will eventually make an expensive mistake.
>
> ---
>
> ### Simplicity is a competitive advantage.
>
> Complex deal structures, complex fee arrangements, and complex communication all benefit the advisor, not the client. Simplicity requires more thought upfront and produces better outcomes.
>
> We write agreements in plain language. We structure deals as simply as the situation allows. We explain things until the client actually understands them. If something is complicated, we ask whether it needs to be.
>
> ---
>
> ### Documentation is a form of respect.
>
> When you document a process, a decision, or an agreement, you are treating the other party's time and trust as something worth preserving. Undocumented agreements create disputes. Undocumented processes create dependence on individuals. Undocumented decisions get relitigated.
>
> The Meritage Operating System exists because we believe this. Knowledge that lives in someone's head leaves with them. Knowledge that's written down belongs to the organization.
>
> ---
>
> ### Good advisors tell clients what they need to hear, not what they want to hear.
>
> A seller who overestimates their business value will be disappointed at market and may blame the advisor. An advisor who reinforces that overestimation to win the engagement has caused the problem.
>
> We'd rather lose an engagement by being honest than win one by being agreeable. Over time, clients who've received honest advice — even when it was uncomfortable — are the ones who refer others.
>
> ---
>
> ### Small teams with high standards outperform large teams with average ones.
>
> Size is not quality. A three-person firm where every person is excellent will consistently outperform a thirty-person firm where most people are adequate. Meritage is built on this premise.
>
> This belief shapes how we hire, how we structure engagements, and how we think about growth. We grow by adding excellent people when they exist, not by filling seats to create the appearance of scale.
>
> ---
>
> ## How These Beliefs Are Used
>
> These beliefs are decision filters. When facing an ambiguous situation, apply them:
>
> - Should we take this engagement? Does it align with long-term trust over short-term revenue?
> - - Should we recommend this deal structure? Is it simple enough to serve the client?
>   - - Should we push this deal toward close? Is the evidence sufficient, or are we assuming?
>     - - Should we say this to the client? Are we telling them what they need to hear?
>      
>       - Beliefs are also hiring filters. We look for people who hold these convictions naturally — not people who will adopt them for a paycheck.
