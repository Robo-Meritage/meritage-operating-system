# Brand Voice

> How Meritage communicates — the words we choose, the tone we use, and the impression we leave.
>
> ---
>
> ## What Brand Voice Is
>
> Brand voice is not a style guide. It's not about fonts or colors. It's about how we sound when we write and speak.
>
> Meritage's brand voice should feel like talking to a senior advisor who's done what you're trying to do — direct, experienced, and on your side. Not a salesperson. Not a lawyer. An operator.
>
> ---
>
> ## The Core Voice Attributes
>
> ### Direct
> We get to the point. We don't bury the lead. The most important thing we have to say goes first, not last.
>
> **Instead of:** "We wanted to reach out because we thought there might be an opportunity to discuss some potential options around your exit strategy..."
> **Write:** "We'd like to discuss buying your business."
>
> ---
>
> ### Credible
> We earn authority by being specific. Vague claims signal insecurity. Specific claims signal experience.
>
> **Instead of:** "We have extensive experience in lower-middle market transactions."
> **Write:** "We've closed transactions from $1M to $18M in enterprise value, primarily in service, healthcare, and logistics businesses."
>
> ---
>
> ### Honest
> We say what's true, not what's comfortable. This includes telling clients when their business isn't worth what they think, when the market isn't right, or when a deal is unlikely to close.
>
> Honesty is a differentiator because most advisors tell clients what they want to hear.
>
> ---
>
> ### Warm
> Direct doesn't mean cold. We're working with people who are selling something they built over decades. That matters. We acknowledge it.
>
> Warm doesn't mean effusive. It means human. It means remembering that behind every transaction is a founder, a family, and a decision that changes their life.
>
> ---
>
> ### Confident
> We don't hedge unnecessarily. "I think" and "it seems like" weaken claims that should be stated firmly.
>
> **Instead of:** "It seems like the market might be seeing increased interest in your sector."
> **Write:** "Buyer interest in this sector is high right now. We're seeing it directly."
>
> ---

> ## What We Are Not
>
> | We are not | We are |
> |---|---|
> | Corporate | Human |
> | Jargon-heavy | Plain-spoken |
> | Inflated | Specific |
> | Passive | Direct |
> | Formal to the point of distance | Professional but accessible |

> ## Writing Guidelines
>
> ### Sentence length
> Variety is important. Short sentences create impact. Longer sentences can carry more nuance and context, but they should never be so long that the reader loses the thread before reaching the end. Mix both.
>
> ### Paragraphs
> Keep them to 3–4 sentences. One idea per paragraph. White space is not wasted space.
>
> ### Word choice
> Favor concrete over abstract. Specific over general. Anglo-Saxon over Latinate.
>
> | Avoid | Use instead |
> |---|---|
> | Utilize | Use |
> | Leverage | Use, apply |
> | Synergies | Shared advantages |
> | Facilitate | Help, enable |
> | Commence | Start |
> | In order to | To |
> | At this point in time | Now |
> | Regarding the matter of | About |
>
> ### Punctuation
> - Em dashes (—) are preferred over semicolons for interruptions and asides
> - - Oxford comma always
>   - - One space after a period (not two)
>     - - Avoid parentheticals — if it's worth saying, say it directly
>

> ## Tone by Channel
>
> ### Outreach emails (cold or warm)
> - Short — under 150 words
> - - Subject line: specific and relevant, not clever
>   - - One clear ask per email
>     - - No attachments on first contact
>      
>       - **Example opener:**
>       - "I work with business owners in [sector] on sell-side transactions. I noticed [specific thing about their company]. Are you open to a 20-minute call this week?"
>      
>       - ### Proposals
>       - - Lead with the client's situation and goals, not Meritage's credentials
>         - - Credentials come after establishing that we understand their problem
> - Use a clear structure: Situation → Approach → Deliverables → Timeline → Investment
> - - No more than 5 pages for initial proposals
>  
>   - ### Client update emails
>   - - Header: what happened this week
>     - - Body: context and implications
>       - - Footer: next steps with owners and due dates
>         - - Tone: clear and calm, even when the situation is complicated
>          
>           - ### LinkedIn / Public content
>           - - Opinion-forward — not just information
>             - - Specific anecdotes over general claims
>               - - No promotional language ("Check out our...", "We're excited to announce...")
>                 - - Every post has a point of view
>                  
>                   - ---
>
> ## Things to Always Avoid
>
> - Exclamation points in professional communication
> - - All-caps for emphasis
>   - - Overuse of bold (bold everything = bold nothing)
>     - - "Please don't hesitate to reach out" (cliché — use "feel free to call" or just end the email)
>       - - "Hope this finds you well" (skip the preamble)
>         - - "Per my last email" (restate the context instead)
>           - - Buzzwords: synergy, ecosystem, best-in-class, value-add, move the needle
>

> ## Editing Checklist
>
> Before sending any external communication:
>
> - [ ] Does the first sentence state the purpose?
> - [ ] - [ ] Is there one clear ask or action?
> - [ ] - [ ] Are there any unnecessary qualifiers?
> - [ ] - [ ] Are there any buzzwords or clichés?
> - [ ] - [ ] Would I be comfortable if this was forwarded?
> - [ ] - [ ] Does this sound like a person, not a company?
