# Executive Manual

>

>
> ## Purpose
>
> This manual captures how I operate as COO — not how Meritage operates as a company. It documents my decision-making framework, weekly cadence, management philosophy, and operating standards so that:
>
> 1. The organization can function predictably without constant direction from me
> 2. 2. New hires and consultants understand what good looks like
>    3. 3. AI tools (Claude, Copilot) have the context they need to assist effectively
>       4. 4. Future me can audit whether present me is operating consistently

>          6. ## Weekly Operating Rhythm
>         
>          7. A strong week has structure. Improvisation happens within the structure, not instead of it.
>
> ### Monday — Direction
> - Review the week's priorities (max 3 high-impact items)
> - - Review open deals, active engagements, and outstanding follow-ups
>   - - Clear the inbox to zero (action, defer, or delete)
>     - - Set the agenda for the week's internal check-ins
>      
>       - ### Tuesday–Thursday — Execution
>       - - Deep work blocks in the morning (no calls before 10am where possible)
>         - - Client calls and meetings in the afternoon
>           - - End of day: update deal tracker and open items list
>            
>             - ### Friday — Review and Reflection
>             - - Review what was completed vs. what was planned
>               - - Write the weekly update for the team
>                 - - Identify one thing that should change next week
>                   - - Prep for any Monday deliverables

> ## Decision-Making Framework
>
> Most bad decisions are made quickly on incomplete information, or slowly on information that was available earlier. The goal is to decide at the right speed with the right information.
>
> ### Decision Types
>
> **Type 1 — Irreversible, high-stakes**
> Take your time. Get input. Document the reasoning. Examples: hiring a senior person, committing to a major client, making a significant purchase.
>
> **Type 2 — Reversible, low-stakes**
> Decide fast. Don't overthink it. A wrong Type 2 decision costs less than a delayed Type 2 decision. Examples: which template to use, which vendor to try, how to structure a meeting.
>
> **Type 3 — Urgent and unclear**
> Buy yourself time. Ask: is this actually urgent, or does it just feel urgent? If truly urgent, decide on best available information and document the reasoning.
>
> ### Before Making a Major Decision
>
> 1. State the decision in one sentence
> 2. 2. List the options (usually 2–4)
>    3. 3. Identify the key criteria
>       4. 4. Note the irreversibility of each option
>          5. 5. Write down what you'd need to believe for each option to be right
>             6. 6. Decide and document
>               
>                7. If you can't state the decision in one sentence, you haven't defined it yet.

>                9. ## Meeting Structure
>
> Meetings have a purpose, an owner, and an outcome. If a meeting doesn't have all three, it shouldn't happen.
>
> ### Meeting Principles
>
> - **Default to async.** If it can be a message, it should be a message.
> - - **Prep is non-negotiable.** Every meeting has an agenda, shared 24 hours in advance where possible.
>   - - **Decisions get documented.** Every meeting that makes a decision produces a one-paragraph summary of the decision and next steps.
>     - - **Time boxes are real.** Meetings start and end on time. Respect for time is respect for people.
>      
>       - ### Standard Meeting Types
>      
>       - **Weekly Team Check-in (30 min)**
>       - Purpose: Alignment, not reporting. What's moving, what's blocked, what needs a decision.
>       - Format: Round-robin updates (60 seconds each), then open issues.
>
> **Deal Review (60 min)**
> Purpose: Review active transactions against milestones.
> Format: Deal-by-deal walkthrough, blockers, next steps, owner.
>
> **Client Call (45–60 min)**
> Purpose: Advance the engagement, build the relationship.
> Format: Agenda sent in advance, recap email within 24 hours.
>
> **One-on-One (30 min)**
> Purpose: Keep individuals unblocked and growing.
> Format: Their agenda first, then mine.

> ## KPI Philosophy
>
> Metrics exist to help us make decisions, not to create reports.
>
> ### Principles
>
> - **Measure what matters.** A KPI that doesn't change behavior is just noise.
> - - **Lead indicators over lag indicators.** Revenue is a lag indicator. Outreach volume, pipeline activity, and proposal conversion are lead indicators.
>   - - **Trend over snapshot.** A single data point tells you nothing. The direction over 4–8 weeks tells you something.
>     - - **Own your numbers.** Every KPI has a single owner. Shared ownership is no ownership.
>      
>       - ### Core KPIs (Meritage)
>      
>       - | Metric | Target | Frequency |
>       - |---|---|---|
>       - | Active engagements | 3–5 | Weekly |
> | Pipeline value (total LOIs in process) | $X | Weekly |
> | Outreach contacts made | X per week | Weekly |
> | Proposal-to-engagement conversion | >50% | Monthly |
> | Days from LOI to close | <90 days | Per deal |
> | NPS / client satisfaction | 9+ | Per engagement |
>
> *Targets to be calibrated as baseline data accumulates.*

> ## Technology Philosophy
>
> Technology should reduce friction, not create it.
>
> ### Principles
>
> - **Buy before build.** If a tool exists that does 80% of what we need, use it. Don't build custom solutions for commodity problems.
> - - **Simple before sophisticated.** A spreadsheet that gets used beats software that doesn't.
>   - - **Consolidate.** Every tool added is a tool that needs to be maintained, paid for, and learned. Fewer tools, used deeply.
>     - - **Document what we use.** The tech stack lives in `docs/technology/`. If it's not documented, it doesn't officially exist.
>      
>       - ### Current Stack Principles
>       - - CRM: Single source of truth for all client and deal data
>         - - Communication: One primary channel per audience (don't fragment)
>           - - Documents: MOS (this repository) for operational knowledge
>             - - Finance: Dedicated tools, not spreadsheets for anything mission-critical
>              
>               - *See `docs/technology/` for the current stack.*

> ## Hiring Philosophy
>
> A-players don't tolerate B-players. Hire slowly, fire quickly.
>
> ### Principles
>
> - **Hire for the role you have, not the role you imagine.** Be specific about the actual job.
> - - **Character over credentials.** Skills can be taught. Judgment, ownership, and work ethic are harder to install.
>   - - **The hiring process is a preview of the working relationship.** Someone who is disorganized during interviews will be disorganized at work.
>     - - **Define "good" before you interview.** Write the scorecard before the first call, not after.
>       - - **Probation is real.** The first 90 days are mutual evaluation. Both sides should be honest about fit.
>        
>         - ### Hiring Process (Standard)
>        
>         - 1. Write the job description (including what "great" looks like)
>           2. 2. Source candidates (referrals first, then external)
>              3. 3. 30-minute screening call (culture and basics)
>                 4. 4. 60-minute structured interview (scorecard-driven)
>                    5. 5. Skills assessment or work sample
>                       6. 6. Reference checks (at least 2)
>                          7. 7. Offer

>                             9. ## Delegation Model
>                            
>                             10. If I'm doing something twice, I should be documenting it. If I'm documenting it, I should be delegating it.
>
> ### The Delegation Test
>
> Before doing anything, ask:
> - Is this the highest-value use of my time right now?
> - - Could someone else do this at 80% of my quality?
>   - - If I delegated this, would I trust the outcome?
>    
>     - If the answer to any of these is "yes, delegate," delegate.
>    
>     - ### Delegation Levels
>    
>     - | Level | Meaning |
> |---|---|
> | L1 | Do it, report when done |
> | L2 | Do it, report immediately if issues arise |
> | L3 | Research options, present recommendation |
> | L4 | Research options, present analysis (I'll decide) |
> | L5 | I'll handle this |
>
> When delegating, always specify the level. "Handle this" is not a delegation — it's an abdication.

> ## Communication Standards
>
> ### Email
> - Subject line describes the ask: "Decision needed: vendor contract" not "Quick question"
> - - First line states the purpose: request, FYI, or update
>   - - Action items are explicit: "Please confirm by Friday" not "Let me know your thoughts"
>     - - Reply the same day for anything marked urgent; within 48 hours for everything else
>      
>       - ### Internal Memos
>       - - Header: To, From, Date, Re
>         - - No longer than one page unless the complexity requires it
>           - - Conclusion before explanation: state the recommendation first, then the reasoning
>            
>             - ### Client Communication
>             - - Response time: same day for active deal clients
>               - - Recap emails after every call: sent within 24 hours
>                 - - Status updates: weekly during active engagements, monthly for pipeline relationships

> ## Operational Standards
>
> ### Documentation
> - If it's not written down, it doesn't exist
> - - Every process, playbook, and decision of significance goes into MOS
>   - - Documents are updated when reality changes — not after the fact
>    
>     - ### Follow-Through
>     - - Every commitment has a deadline and an owner
>       - - If a deadline can't be met, communicate before it's missed — not after
>         - - "I'll get back to you" is a commitment. Treat it as one.
>          
>           - ### Quality
>           - - Good enough, done beats perfect, pending
>             - - But "good enough" has a floor: accurate, clear, and professional
>               - - Rework is always more expensive than getting it right the first time
