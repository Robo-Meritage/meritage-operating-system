# Module 04: Valuation

> How Meritage determines, frames, and communicates a Company's Enterprise Value range — and why this is the most consequential moment in the engagement.
>
> ---
>
> ## Purpose
>
> Valuation is not a calculation. It is a judgment supported by analysis.
>
> This module establishes how Meritage develops, communicates, and defends an Enterprise Value range for a lower middle market company. The valuation produced here is an advisory estimate — not a certified appraisal, not an investment banking opinion of value, and not a guarantee. It is a professional assessment informed by financial normalization, comparable transaction data, market conditions, and the Principal Advisor's experience with what buyers in this sector will actually pay.
>
> The valuation produced in this module serves three purposes: it informs Seller expectations before the process begins, it guides the GTM pricing and positioning strategy, and it establishes the floor below which Meritage recommends against accepting an offer. Every downstream decision — which buyers to approach, how to position the company, how to evaluate IOIs, how to respond to LOI terms — flows from this foundation.
>
> If the valuation is wrong, the engagement suffers. If the Seller's expectations are misaligned from the beginning, no amount of execution quality in later modules will fix it. This is why valuation comes before positioning, before GTM, before buyer outreach, and before any external communication about the deal.
>
> ---
>
> ## Philosophy
>
> **Valuation is the moment we earn trust or lose it.**
>
> The Meritage valuation conversation is not a negotiation with the Seller. It is an honest presentation of what the market is likely to pay, grounded in data and informed by experience. We do not inflate valuations to win engagements. We do not anchor to a number the Seller wants to hear. We present what we believe the market will bear, explain our reasoning, and help the Seller decide whether it makes sense to proceed.
>
> This position is non-negotiable. Inflated valuations create misaligned Sellers, which create failed processes, which damage Meritage's reputation and waste everyone's time. A Seller who is surprised at the LOI stage — because the valuation conversation was too optimistic — is a Seller who will blame their advisor. We would rather lose the engagement than set a Seller up for disappointment.
>
> **EBITDA is a starting point, not the answer.**
>
> Lower middle market buyers do not pay for reported earnings. They pay for normalized, defensible, recurring earnings. The Meritage valuation process begins with financial normalization: identifying and adjusting for owner addbacks, one-time items, non-recurring expenses, and above- or below-market compensation. Until this work is complete, we cannot responsibly state a valuation range.
>
> Equally important: EBITDA is not the only driver of value. A company generating $2M in EBITDA with a sole-owner dependency, customer concentration, and no management team will not trade at the same multiple as a company with the same EBITDA, a tenured leadership team, diversified revenue, and documented processes. Operational readiness — the degree to which the business can run and grow without the current owner — is a primary multiple driver in the lower middle market.
>
> **Multiple selection is a judgment call, not a formula.**
>
> Comparable transaction databases provide ranges. They do not provide answers. The appropriate multiple for a given company depends on factors that cannot be reduced to a formula: the quality of the management team, the stickiness of customer relationships, the defensibility of margins, growth trajectory, industry tailwinds or headwinds, and what buyers are currently paying attention to. Meritage applies experience, not just data, to determine where within a range a company should be positioned.
>
> **We position for the right buyer, not the highest number.**
>
> The valuation we develop is not a listing price. It is an internal anchor that guides positioning and negotiation. In the market, we describe value in terms of the opportunity — the growth potential, the platform potential for a strategic buyer, the cash flow stability for a financial buyer. We do not lead with a number. We lead with a story, and the number supports the story.
>
> **Client expectation management begins here.**
>
> The valuation conversation is also the first serious test of the Seller's readiness to sell. A Seller who cannot accept a data-supported valuation range, who insists on a number unsupported by comparable transactions, or who anchors irrationally to a round number or a multiple they heard at a conference is a Seller who is not ready. Meritage takes the position that we would rather have this conversation before signing an Engagement Agreement than at the LOI stage.
>

>
> **In scope**
>
> - Financial normalization and EBITDA adjustment (addback analysis)
> - - Comparable transaction research and multiple analysis
>   - - Valuation range development (low, mid, target)
>     - - Internal valuation memo for advisory use
>       - - Seller-facing valuation presentation
>         - - Alignment conversation with Seller on valuation expectations
>           - - Identification of value drivers and detractors
>             - - Assessment of operational readiness as a multiple driver
>               - - Identification of deal-level risk factors that affect valuation
>                
>                 - **Out of scope**
>                
>                 - - Certified business appraisal (requires a licensed appraiser)
>                   - - Tax opinions of value (requires a CPA or tax attorney)
>                     - - Legal opinions of value (requires licensed legal counsel)
>                       - - Post-LOI valuation disputes or quality-of-earnings adjustments (those belong to due diligence)
>                         - - Buyer-side valuation analysis (Meritage represents Sellers)
>                           - - Guaranteeing a sale at the stated valuation range

> ## Inputs
>
> The following must be in place before valuation analysis begins:
>
> - Three years of financial statements (P&L and balance sheet, CPA-prepared preferred)
> - - Three years of business tax returns
>   - - Owner compensation schedule (all W-2s, K-1s, and 1099s paid to owner and owner family members)
>     - - Preliminary addback schedule from Seller (all proposed adjustments with supporting documentation)
>       - - Industry classification (SIC/NAICS code, confirmed sector and sub-sector)
>         - - Preliminary understanding of customer concentration, contract status, and revenue mix
>           - - Signed Engagement Agreement or confirmed intent to engage (valuation analysis is not performed speculatively for unengaged prospects)
>            
>             - ---
>
> ## Process
>
> ### Step 1: Financial Normalization
>
> Begin with the most recently completed fiscal year. Work backward three years. For each year, reconstruct EBITDA by starting with reported net income and adding back: interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization, and then applying normalized adjustments.
>
> Normalized adjustments are divided into three categories:
>
> **Owner addbacks:** Compensation, benefits, personal expenses, and perquisites paid through the business to the owner or owner's family that a buyer would not incur or would incur at market rates. Each addback must be documented and defensible — a buyer's quality-of-earnings team will scrutinize every line.
>
> **One-time or non-recurring items:** Legal settlements, one-time consulting fees, extraordinary losses, COVID-related expenses or benefits, and similar items that do not represent the ongoing earning capacity of the business. These must be documented with evidence.
>
> **Run-rate adjustments:** Changes to the cost structure that are already in place or contractually committed. Run-rate adjustments require more scrutiny than addbacks and should only be included when the change is already effective or contractually locked.
>
> The output of Step 1 is an Adjusted EBITDA figure for each of the three years, and a trailing twelve-month (TTM) Adjusted EBITDA if different from the most recent fiscal year.
>
> ### Step 2: Comparable Transaction Research
>
> Research comparable transactions using available databases and advisor experience. Identify transactions in the same or adjacent industry, similar revenue scale, and similar EBITDA margin profile.
>
> Record the range of EBITDA multiples observed in comparable transactions. Note the date of each transaction — multiples from 2019–2021 may not reflect current market conditions.
>
> Layer in current market intelligence: what are financial buyers currently paying in this sector? What are strategic buyers paying? Are there sector-specific dynamics that expand or compress multiples?
>
> The output of Step 2 is a documented multiple range with commentary on why the range is what it is.
>
> ### Step 3: Value Driver and Detractor Assessment
>
> Before applying a multiple, assess where within the range this specific company should sit.
>
> **Positive value drivers:**
> - Tenured management team that will stay post-close
> - - Diversified customer base (no single customer >15–20% of revenue)
>   - - Recurring or contracted revenue
>     - - Documented processes and systems
>       - - Positive EBITDA growth trend (3-year)
>         - - Defensible competitive moat
>           - - Clean financials, minimal restatement risk
>             - - Owner not required for day-to-day operations
>              
>               - **Value detractors (multiple compressors):**
>               - - Sole-owner dependency
>                 - - Customer concentration
>                   - - Declining revenue or margin trend
>                     - - Undocumented or owner-dependent customer relationships
>                       - - Deferred capital expenditures or deferred maintenance
>                         - - Key-person risk beyond the owner
>                           - - Pending litigation, regulatory exposure, or environmental liability
>                             - - Financial statements that require significant normalization
>                              
>                               - The output of Step 3 is a written value driver summary.
>                              
>                               - ### Step 4: Valuation Range Development
>                              
>                               - Apply the selected multiple range to Adjusted EBITDA to produce a preliminary Enterprise Value range. Express this as three figures:
>
> - **Floor:** The minimum acceptable Enterprise Value. Below this number, Meritage recommends against accepting an offer.
> - - **Target:** The value we will position for in the market and anchor buyer conversations around.
>   - - **Stretch:** A realistic upside scenario, achievable with the right buyer and market conditions.
>    
>     - Note: Enterprise Value is not the same as the Seller's proceeds. Adjustments for debt, working capital, transaction costs, and earnout structures will affect net proceeds.
>    
>     - ### Step 5: Seller Valuation Presentation
>    
>     - Prepare a Seller-facing valuation summary. The presentation should:
> - Lead with the financial normalization so the Seller can see and agree to the numbers
> - - Explain the multiple range and the factors that place this company within it
>   - - Present the valuation range as an estimate, not a guarantee
>     - - Address Seller expectations directly
>      
>       - The conversation accompanying this presentation is not a negotiation. Meritage presents what the data supports and invites the Seller to ask questions.
>

> ## Standards
>
> **What excellent looks like in this module:**
>
> A Meritage valuation package is complete, defensible, and Seller-ready within 10 business days of receiving complete financial information. It contains a documented addback schedule with every adjustment supported by evidence, a comparable transaction summary with sources cited, a written value driver narrative that is specific to this company, a three-tier Enterprise Value range with rationale, and a Seller presentation that a first-time Seller can understand.
>
> The Principal Advisor can defend every line of the addback schedule. Every addback is labeled as: Confirmed, Probable, or Disputed. Disputed addbacks are not included in the headline Adjusted EBITDA figure without a clear notation.
>
> **Minimum acceptable quality:**
>
> - All three years of financials are normalized, not just the most recent year
> - - Every owner addback is documented by category and dollar amount
>   - - The comparable transaction summary identifies at least three transactions with stated multiples
>     - - The Enterprise Value range has a written rationale — not just a number
>       - - The Seller has received and reviewed the valuation presentation before the engagement is launched
>        
>         - **Red flags that indicate this stage is not complete:**
>        
>         - - Valuation is based on a single year of financials
>           - - Addbacks are undocumented or based solely on Seller assertions
>             - - The multiple was selected without reference to comparable transactions
>               - - The Seller's stated value expectation has not been explicitly addressed
>                 - - The team is proceeding to positioning without Seller alignment on valuation
>

> ## Common Failure Modes
>
> **1. Taking the engagement before doing the valuation.**
> This is the most common and most damaging failure mode. Advisors sign an Engagement Agreement, collect a retainer, and then discover the Seller's expectations are 2–3x what the market will pay. The proper sequence is: preliminary valuation discussion before engagement, detailed valuation analysis before positioning and GTM launch.
>
> **2. Inflating the valuation to win the client.**
> An inflated valuation is not a competitive advantage. It is a liability. A Seller who goes to market with an expectation of $15M and receives IOIs in the $8–10M range will blame the advisor.
>
> **3. Treating EBITDA normalization as a formality.**
> Financial normalization is analytical work. Addbacks that cannot be defended will be challenged — and reversed — during quality of earnings. An addback schedule that does not survive buyer scrutiny creates post-LOI renegotiation risk and can kill a deal.
>
> **4. Ignoring operational readiness as a multiple driver.**
> The multiple is not determined by EBITDA alone. Two companies with identical EBITDA will trade at very different multiples if one has a management team and documented processes and the other is sole-owner dependent.
>
> **5. Skipping the Seller alignment conversation.**
> Presenting a valuation in writing without a live conversation is a mistake. A Seller who receives a valuation memo via email and does not respond is not aligned — they are avoidant.
>
> **6. Conflating Enterprise Value with Seller proceeds.**
> Enterprise Value is not what the Seller receives — it is the total value of the business before debt payoff, working capital adjustments, transaction costs, and deferred consideration. Advisors who present Enterprise Value without walking through the net proceeds calculation create Sellers who feel deceived at closing.
>
> **7. Using stale comparable transactions.**
> A comp from 2021 does not reflect a 2024 or 2025 market. Advisors must use current transaction data or explicitly discount older comps.

> ## AI Assistance
>
> **AI can assist with:**
>
> - Researching publicly available comparable transactions and reported multiple ranges by industry
> - - Formatting and organizing EBITDA normalization schedules for review
>   - - Drafting the Seller valuation narrative (not the numbers — the explanatory text)
>     - - Summarizing the logic of the addback schedule in plain language for Seller presentation
>       - - Identifying value driver and detractor categories based on company description
>         - - Generating first drafts of the Seller valuation presentation for advisor review
>           - - Checking that the valuation memo is complete against the standards defined in this module
>             - - Cross-referencing definitions and terms against the Domain Model glossary
>              
>               - **AI should not:**
>              
>               - - Determine or recommend the valuation range
>                 - - Select the EBITDA multiple
>                   - - Advise the Seller on whether an offer price is acceptable
>                     - - Classify addbacks without advisor review
>                       - - Interpret or qualify financial statements — this requires a CPA
>                         - - Represent any output as a certified appraisal or fairness opinion
>                           - - Make contact with buyers or share any valuation information externally
