# Module 05: Positioning

> How Meritage transforms a valuation into a market narrative — and why this is the bridge between finance and strategy.
>
> ---
>
> ## Purpose
>
> Positioning is the stage between Valuation and Go-to-Market. It answers a fundamentally different question than valuation does.
>
> Valuation answers: *What is this business worth?*
>
> Positioning answers: *Why should a specific buyer pay more for this business than another buyer would?*
>
> These are not the same question. A business with $2M in EBITDA trading at a 6x multiple produces the same Enterprise Value whether it is positioned as a platform acquisition for a roll-up or as a steady cash flow vehicle for a financial buyer. But the buyers are different, the story is different, the materials are different, and the probability of closing at the target price is different.
>
> Positioning is the strategic layer that connects the financial analysis to the market. Without it, GTM is execution without direction. With it, every downstream decision — buyer list, CIM narrative, Management Call talking points, IOI evaluation — flows from a coherent investment thesis.
>
> This module is where Meritage's advisory value is most differentiated. Any competent firm can normalize EBITDA. The ability to construct a compelling, specific, and defensible investment narrative for a lower middle market business — one that speaks to a particular buyer's strategic goals — is the skill that separates advisors.
>
> ---
>
> ## Philosophy
>
> **Positioning is not marketing. It is strategic analysis expressed in buyer language.**
>
> The investment thesis for a company is not a press release. It is a structured argument — grounded in the company's actual competitive position, financial performance, and market opportunity — that explains why a specific category of buyer should value this business more than the financial fundamentals alone suggest. That argument must be defensible. It will be tested in Management Calls, challenged in due diligence, and scrutinized at the LOI stage.
>
> **Different buyers pay for different things.**
>
> A private equity firm buying a platform company values optionality and scalability. A strategic buyer values market share, customer relationships, or the ability to eliminate a competitor or enter a new geography. A family office or independent sponsor values cash flow stability and management team quality. An employee stock ownership plan values continuity and culture. Positioning means identifying which buyer type will pay the most for this specific business — and why — before a single outreach call is made.
>
> **The investment thesis must be honest, not aspirational.**
>
> A positioning narrative built on claims the business cannot support will collapse in diligence. Buyers will find the gaps. The Seller will be exposed. Meritage's reputation will be damaged. The investment thesis must be grounded in facts that can be verified. This does not mean the narrative should be conservative — it means the narrative should be precise. We tell the true story of the business as compellingly as possible. We do not invent a story the business cannot sustain.
>
> **Risk framing is part of positioning, not a concession.**
>
> Every business has risks. A sophisticated buyer knows this. An investment narrative that does not acknowledge risks signals either naivety or bad faith. Meritage's approach is to identify the material risks, frame them honestly, and where possible include a mitigation narrative. A risk that is acknowledged and explained is less damaging than a risk that the buyer discovers and the advisor never mentioned.
>
> **Positioning is completed before GTM begins. It is not GTM.**
>
> The positioning work in this module produces a strategic brief — an internal document that informs the CIM, the Teaser, the buyer list, and the Management Call preparation. It is not the CIM itself. The CIM is the execution of the positioning strategy. The positioning strategy must be completed and approved before GTM work begins.
>

> ## Process
>
> ### Step 1: Buyer Universe Analysis
>
> Before constructing the investment thesis, identify who will buy this business and why.
>
> Segment the potential buyer universe into categories:
>
> **Financial buyers** — private equity firms, family offices, independent sponsors, search funds. These buyers value EBITDA, cash flow consistency, management team quality, and growth potential. They are building portfolios, not operating businesses. They pay for stability and upside.
>
> **Strategic buyers** — companies in the same or adjacent industry that would benefit from the acquisition through market share, geographic expansion, product line extension, talent acquisition, or competitive elimination. Strategic buyers often pay more than financial buyers because the acquisition generates synergies that do not appear in the standalone financial model.
>
> **Owner-operators** — individual buyers seeking to acquire an operating business, often through an SBA loan or seller financing. They pay less than PE or strategic buyers, but they are a relevant universe for businesses below a certain EBITDA threshold or with characteristics that make institutional buyers less interested.
>
> **ESOPs** — employee stock ownership plans. Relevant for Sellers who prioritize employee continuity and culture preservation. ESOP transactions have specific structural requirements and are not appropriate for all businesses.
>
> For each category, assess: Is this buyer type likely to be interested in this business? What would this buyer pay? What would they need to see to get comfortable? What risks would give them pause?
>
> The output of Step 1 is a ranked buyer persona list that identifies the primary, secondary, and tertiary buyer categories for this engagement, with a rationale for each ranking.
>
> ### Step 2: Investment Thesis Development
>
> The investment thesis is a structured argument, not a tagline. It answers four questions:
>
> 1. **What does this business do, in plain language?** Not the legal definition or the owner's self-description — the functional description a sophisticated buyer would use.
>
> 2. 2. **Why is this business valuable to the right buyer?** Not "because it generates EBITDA" — that is true of every business. The specific reasons this business, in this industry, at this stage, generates above-average returns for a qualified buyer.
>   
>    3. 3. **What is the growth opportunity?** Not aspirational claims — specific, supportable avenues for value creation: geographic expansion, product adjacency, management talent addition, technology adoption, market consolidation. The buyer needs to believe there is money to be made beyond the current run rate.
>      
>       4. 4. **Why now?** What makes this the right moment for a transaction? This can include: owner retirement, market cycle timing, inbound buyer interest, platform scarcity in the sector, or competitive dynamics that make this an attractive window.
>         
>          5. The investment thesis is 3-5 paragraphs. It is the foundation for every external communication.
>         
>          6. ### Step 3: Strategic Fit Analysis by Buyer Category
>         
>          7. For each buyer category identified in Step 1, develop a tailored fit hypothesis:
>
> - What does this buyer gain from the acquisition that they cannot easily build or buy elsewhere?
> - - What synergies exist — cost reduction, revenue enhancement, capability acquisition, market access?
>   - - What is the likely integration model — acqui-hire, platform add-on, standalone operation, carve-out?
>     - - What is the buyer's valuation logic — pure EBITDA multiple, synergy-adjusted, strategic premium?
>      
>       - This analysis determines how the CIM will be customized for different buyer categories and which buyers receive the full CIM versus a targeted summary.
>      
>       - ### Step 4: Risk Identification and Mitigation Framing
>      
>       - Every positioning brief must include an honest risk register. The risks identified here will inform the CIM, Management Call preparation, and due diligence management.
>
> For each identified risk, document:
> - **The risk:** What is the actual exposure?
> - - **The evidence:** What data or observation supports this as a risk?
>   - - **The mitigation:** What has been done, is being done, or can be done to reduce this risk?
>     - - **The framing:** How will Meritage present this risk to buyers in a way that is honest but not alarmist?
>      
>       - Common risk categories in lower middle market businesses:
>       - - Sole-owner dependency
>         - - Customer concentration
>           - - Key employee retention
>             - - Revenue trend (declining or lumpy)
>               - - Deferred capital expenditure
>                 - - Pending litigation or regulatory exposure
>                   - - Market headwinds in the industry
>                     - - Financial statement quality concerns
>                      
>                       - ### Step 5: Positioning Brief
>                      
>                       - Compile the outputs of Steps 1-4 into an internal Positioning Brief. This document is for Meritage's use and informs GTM preparation. It is not distributed to buyers.
>                      
>                       - The Positioning Brief includes:
> - Investment thesis (3-5 paragraphs)
> - - Buyer persona ranking with rationale
>   - - Strategic fit hypotheses by buyer category
>     - - Growth narrative summary
>       - - Risk register with mitigation framing
>         - - Operational maturity assessment
>           - - Key differentiators (what makes this business defensible or distinctive)
>             - - Seller-specific deal considerations (earnout tolerance, transition availability, legacy preferences)
>              
>               - The Positioning Brief is reviewed with the Seller for factual accuracy. The Seller does not need to approve the strategic framing — that is Meritage's judgment — but any factual claims about the business must be confirmed.
>

> ## Standards
>
> **What excellent looks like in this module:**
>
> An excellent Positioning Brief is specific to this business, in this industry, at this moment in the market. It cannot be mistaken for the positioning brief of any other engagement. Every statement in the investment thesis is supportable by a specific fact about this business. The buyer persona ranking reflects actual knowledge of who is buying in this sector, not a generic list. The risk register is honest and complete — it does not omit known risks, and it includes a mitigation position for every material risk.
>
> The Principal Advisor can walk a Seller through the Positioning Brief and explain why every decision was made. The Seller can confirm every factual claim. The investment thesis has been tested against the "Goldman Sachs test": if this sentence could apply equally well to Goldman Sachs, a regional broker, and Meritage, it is not specific enough.
>
> **Minimum acceptable quality:**
>
> - Investment thesis is 3-5 paragraphs with at least three specific, verifiable claims about the business
> - - Buyer persona ranking identifies at least two buyer categories with distinct strategic rationale for each
>   - - Risk register addresses all material risks identified during valuation
>     - - The Seller has reviewed and confirmed the factual accuracy of the Positioning Brief before GTM begins
>       - - The Positioning Brief is complete before any GTM materials are drafted
>        
>         - **Red flags that indicate this stage is not complete:**
>        
>         - - The investment thesis could apply to any business in the industry (not specific enough)
>           - - No buyer persona has been defined — the buyer list is "anyone who might be interested"
>             - - Known risks from the valuation stage have not been addressed in the risk register
>               - - GTM materials (CIM, Teaser) are being drafted before the Positioning Brief is complete
>                 - - The growth narrative is aspirational without factual support

> ## Common Failure Modes
>
> **1. Skipping positioning entirely and going straight to the CIM.**
> This is the most common failure mode. Advisors who move directly from valuation to CIM preparation are executing without strategy. The result is a CIM that describes the business accurately but does not compel any specific buyer to act. Generic CIMs produce generic IOIs at the low end of the range.
>
> **2. Confusing the positioning narrative with marketing copy.**
> Positioning is a strategic argument. Marketing copy is language designed to generate interest. The investment thesis is not a brochure. It is an analysis. Advisors who write enthusiastic but vague investment narratives ("leading provider," "significant growth opportunity," "strong management team") have not done the positioning work — they have produced filler.
>
> **3. Building the buyer list before defining buyer personas.**
> A buyer list built without buyer personas is a contact list, not a strategy. Advisors who compile buyers by searching databases without first asking "who would pay the most for this and why" will approach buyers who are not qualified, miss buyers who are, and waste the most valuable resource in the process: the Seller's willingness to accept outreach fatigue.
>
> **4. Ignoring the Seller's deal priorities in the positioning strategy.**
> A Seller who wants to minimize earn-out exposure should not be positioned primarily to PE buyers who will demand performance-based consideration. A Seller who cares about employee continuity should not be positioned to buyers with known track records of eliminating headcount post-close. The positioning strategy must integrate the Seller's actual priorities — not just the financial terms.
>
> **5. Treating risk framing as optional.**
> Advisors who do not develop a risk mitigation position before going to market will be caught flat-footed when buyers raise those risks in Management Calls. The question is not whether risks will be raised — they will be raised. The question is whether Meritage has already developed a confident, honest position on each risk, or whether the Seller will be surprised by the question.
>
> **6. Positioning for a buyer who won't appear.**
> In some sectors, the "obvious" strategic buyer has already made acquisitions, exited the market, or is not currently active. Advisors must have current market intelligence, not historical assumptions. A positioning strategy built around a buyer category that is not currently active in M&A will generate outreach activity with no conversions.

> ## AI Assistance
>
> **AI can assist with:**
>
> - Researching strategic buyer activity in the sector (recent acquisitions, stated growth strategies, press releases)
> - - Drafting the investment thesis from a structured fact set provided by the advisor
>   - - Generating buyer persona templates with key questions to answer per category
>     - - Drafting synergy hypothesis frameworks by buyer type
>       - - Identifying risk categories common to businesses in this sector
>         - - Formatting the Positioning Brief structure
>           - - Cross-referencing the positioning narrative against domain definitions for consistency
>             - - Reviewing the investment thesis against the "Goldman Sachs test" — flagging language that is too generic
>              
>               - **AI should not:**
>              
>               - - Determine which buyer category to prioritize — that requires market knowledge and advisor judgment
>                 - - Generate the investment thesis from publicly available company information alone — the thesis must incorporate non-public deal intelligence
>                   - - Assess whether a specific buyer is currently active — that requires current market intelligence from the advisor
>                     - - Approve the positioning strategy — the Principal Advisor owns that judgment
>                       - - Draft the Positioning Brief without advisor-provided inputs on buyer universe, deal priorities, and risk factors
