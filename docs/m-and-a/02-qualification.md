# Module 02: Qualification

> The decision engine that determines whether Meritage should commit its time and reputation to an engagement.
>
> ---
>
> ## Purpose
>
> Qualification is not a step in the process — it is a gate. It is the moment Meritage decides whether to invest its advisory capacity, its reputation, and its relationships in an engagement. Every failed engagement, every wasted process, every misaligned Seller relationship can be traced to a qualification failure: taking a client who was not ready, taking a client whose expectations could not be met, or taking a client because the retainer was appealing rather than because the engagement was sound.
>
> The purpose of this module is to make the qualification decision rigorous, consistent, and documented. It answers four questions, in order:
>
> 1. **Should we take this client?** — Is this a business Meritage can successfully represent?
> 2. 2. **Can we successfully represent them?** — Does the Seller have the qualities that make a representation relationship productive?
>    3. 3. **What work is required before going to market?** — If we take this engagement, what must happen first?
>       4. 4. **If we say no, why?** — Document the decline reason so institutional knowledge accumulates.
>         
>          5. Qualification ends with one of three outcomes: **proceed to engagement**, **decline**, or **take with conditions** (defined pre-market requirements that must be met before the engagement launches).
>         
>          6. ---
>         
>          7. ## Philosophy
>
> **Meritage's most important no is the one said in this module.**
>
> Every hour spent on an unqualified engagement is an hour taken from a qualified one. Meritage is not a volume business. The value of the Meritage approach depends on focused, high-quality representation of businesses that are ready to go to market and priced to close. Taking unqualified clients dilutes that value — for Meritage, for the Seller, and for the buyers who will eventually see Meritage's deal flow.
>
> **The qualification decision is an advisory act, not a sales act.**
>
> Advisors who treat qualification as a sales process — looking for reasons to say yes rather than reasons to say no — will build a pipeline of failed engagements. The qualification conversation is where Meritage earns the Seller's trust by being honest about what the process requires and whether this business is ready for it. A Seller who hears an honest no from Meritage, and understands why, may become a client two years later — after they've addressed the issues Meritage identified.
>
> **Qualification protects the Seller as much as it protects Meritage.**
>
> Going to market before a business is ready exposes the Seller to damaged credibility, burned buyer relationships, and a lower price. Once a business has been through a failed process, the next process is harder. Meritage's qualification discipline is, in part, a service to Sellers who do not fully understand what happens when a deal fails.
>
> **The Meritage 5P Framework is the primary evaluation structure.**
>
> Every qualification assessment uses the 5P Framework: People, Performance, Position, Potential, Preparedness. The framework is defined in `docs/frameworks/meritage-5p-framework.md`. This module describes how to apply it.
>

> ## Process
>
> ### Step 1: Pre-Meeting Research
>
> Before the qualification conversation, the Principal Advisor conducts basic research on the prospect:
>
> - Business: industry, approximate size, years in operation, geographic footprint
> - - Owner: background, any public information, referral context
>   - - Market: current M&A activity in the sector, comparable transactions, buyer universe
>     - - Red flags: any public information suggesting litigation, regulatory issues, or known distress
>      
>       - The purpose of pre-meeting research is not to form a conclusion — it is to ensure the discovery conversation is informed and focused on the areas that need clarification, not on background that can be researched in advance.
>      
>       - ### Step 2: Qualification Discovery Conversation
>      
>       - The qualification conversation is a structured interview, not a sales pitch. Meritage is evaluating the prospect. The prospect is also evaluating Meritage, but Meritage's evaluation takes priority — the goal is to reach an honest assessment of fit, not to close the engagement.
>
> The discovery conversation follows the structure in `docs/templates/qualification-discovery-questionnaire.md`. It covers all five lenses of the 5P Framework:
>
> - **People:** Who owns the business? Who is on the leadership team? What is driving the decision to sell? What does the owner expect from the process?
> - - **Performance:** What is the revenue and EBITDA trajectory? What are the owner's addbacks? Are financials CPA-prepared?
>   - - **Position:** What does the business do? Why do customers choose it? What is the competitive landscape?
>     - - **Potential:** Where has growth been left on the table? What would a new owner do differently?
>       - - **Preparedness:** How organized is the financial information? Has the owner consulted a CPA or attorney? What is the timeline expectation?
>        
>         - The discovery conversation ends with a preliminary discussion of valuation expectations. This is not a valuation analysis — it is an expectations check. Meritage presents a rough range of where the business is likely to trade based on what was learned in the conversation, and observes how the Seller responds.
>        
>         - ### Step 3: 5P Assessment
>        
>         - Following the discovery conversation, the Principal Advisor completes a qualitative 5P assessment, rating each lens as:
>
> - **Strong** — This dimension is a genuine asset. It will support the engagement.
> - - **Adequate** — This dimension meets minimum requirements. It will not impede the engagement.
>   - - **Weak** — This dimension has concerns. It may require pre-market work or will create challenges during the process.
>     - - **Disqualifying** — This dimension has characteristics that, in the advisor's judgment, make a successful engagement unlikely or inadvisable.
>      
>       - A single Disqualifying rating in any lens is sufficient to decline or require conditions. Multiple Weak ratings without a clear remediation path should produce the same outcome.
>      
>       - ### Step 4: Qualification Score
>      
>       - The Principal Advisor completes the Qualification Scorecard (see `docs/templates/client-qualification-scorecard.md`). The score is on a 100-point scale across six weighted categories:
>
> | Category | Weight | What it measures |
> |---|---|---|
> | Financial Quality | 20 pts | Defensibility and quality of financial statements and addbacks |
> | Seller Readiness | 20 pts | Owner motivation, expectations alignment, advisor relationship quality |
> | Operational Maturity | 20 pts | Management depth, process documentation, owner independence |
> | Market Attractiveness | 15 pts | Sector dynamics, buyer universe, comparable transaction activity |
> | Growth Potential | 15 pts | Identifiable value creation opportunities for a buyer |
> | Transaction Readiness | 10 pts | Documentation quality, data room readiness, professional advisor engagement |
>
> **Score interpretation:**
>
> | Score | Interpretation |
> |---|---|
> | 80–100 | Proceed — strong candidate for engagement |
> | 65–79 | Proceed with caution — address identified weaknesses before or early in the engagement |
> | 50–64 | Conditional — specific pre-market requirements must be met before proceeding |
> | Below 50 | Decline — engagement is unlikely to succeed at this time |
>
> The score informs the decision. It does not replace advisor judgment. An advisor may proceed with a score below 65 if a specific weakness is addressable and the prospect is committed to addressing it. An advisor may decline a score above 80 if there is a disqualifying factor not captured in the score — such as a cultural fit concern or an undisclosed legal issue.
>
> ### Step 5: Qualification Decision
>
> The Principal Advisor makes one of three decisions:
>
> **Proceed to Engagement:** The business meets Meritage's qualification criteria. The advisor is confident Meritage can add value and produce a successful outcome. Proceed to Module 03 (Engagement).
>
> **Take with Conditions:** The business has genuine potential but requires specific pre-market work before Meritage can engage. The advisor communicates the conditions clearly, in writing, and establishes a timeline for reassessment. Examples: *"We want to represent you once you have three years of CPA-prepared financials"* or *"We recommend addressing the customer concentration issue before going to market."*
>
> **Decline:** The business does not meet Meritage's qualification criteria, or the advisor does not have sufficient confidence in the engagement's likelihood of success. The decline is communicated respectfully, with a clear explanation, and with a referral to appropriate resources where possible.
>
> ### Step 6: Documentation
>
> Every qualification assessment is documented — regardless of outcome. The documentation includes:
> - 5P Assessment ratings with notes
> - - Qualification Score with category breakdowns
>   - - Decision and rationale
>     - - If conditional: specific conditions and timeline
>       - - If declined: decline reason (from the objective decline reasons below)
>        
>         - Documentation serves two purposes: it creates accountability in the qualification decision, and it builds the institutional knowledge that improves future qualification accuracy.
>

>
> ## Question 1: Should We Take This Client?
>
> The primary evaluation filters are:
>
> **Financial fit:** Does the business generate sufficient EBITDA to produce a transaction in Meritage's target range? Is the financial performance defensible and normalizable?
>
> **Market fit:** Is there an active buyer market for this type of business? Are there comparable transactions that establish a realistic valuation range?
>
> **Seller motivation:** Is the owner genuinely motivated to complete a transaction at a realistic price within a realistic timeline?
>
> **Operational maturity:** Can the business operate without the owner? Is there a management team that will survive the process and continue post-close?
>
> **Likelihood of a successful exit:** Given the business characteristics, the Seller's expectations, and the current market, what is Meritage's honest assessment of the probability of a successful close?
>
> **Cultural fit:** Is this a relationship Meritage wants? Will the Seller take advice, share information honestly, and be a constructive partner through a difficult process?
>
> **Advisor confidence:** Does the Principal Advisor believe this engagement will result in a successful outcome? If the honest answer is no or uncertain, the default is to decline.
>
> ---
>
> ## Question 2: Can We Successfully Represent Them?
>
> Beyond the business itself, Meritage evaluates whether the Seller relationship will be productive:
>
> **Quality of financials:** Are CPA-prepared financials available? If not, is the Seller willing and able to produce them before going to market?
>
> **Responsiveness:** During the qualification process itself, has the prospect been responsive, organized, and forthcoming? A prospect who is difficult during qualification will be more difficult during the engagement.
>
> **Expectations alignment:** Has the prospect demonstrated an ability to engage with market data and revise their expectations accordingly? Or do they hold rigidly to a target price regardless of evidence?
>
> **Undisclosed issues:** Has the prospect disclosed all material information about the business — including litigation, regulatory exposure, environmental issues, and customer concentration? A prospect who withholds information during qualification will create problems during due diligence.
>
> **Willingness to accept advice:** Does the prospect listen, ask questions, and engage with Meritage's recommendations? Or do they debate every observation and reject advice that conflicts with their preferred narrative?
>
> **Owner dependence:** Is the owner aware of the owner-dependence issue and willing to work on it? A Seller who insists the business runs fine without them, when it clearly does not, is not ready.
>
> ---
>
> ## Question 3: What Work Is Required Before Going to Market?
>
> For engagements that are viable but not immediately ready, Meritage identifies pre-market requirements:
>
> **Financial cleanup:** Ensuring three years of CPA-prepared financials are available, addbacks are documented, and any financial irregularities are resolved.
>
> **Addback documentation:** Building the addback schedule with supporting documentation before the engagement begins, so it is ready for buyer scrutiny from day one.
>
> **Leadership depth:** Identifying key management gaps and developing a plan to address them — whether through retention packages, a new hire, or a defined transition plan.
>
> **Customer diversification:** Where a single customer represents excessive concentration, developing a plan to reduce that concentration before going to market — or at minimum, documenting it with a mitigation narrative.
>
> **Growth story development:** Where the growth narrative is thin, working with the Seller to identify and document the growth levers that a buyer can execute on.
>
> **Operational improvements:** Addressing specific operational issues — deferred maintenance, undocumented processes, missing vendor contracts — that will surface in due diligence.
>
> **Data room readiness:** Ensuring the basic documentation a buyer will request is organized and accessible before the process begins, not assembled under time pressure during due diligence.
>
> Pre-market requirements are communicated in writing as conditions of engagement. They are specific, measurable, and time-bound.
>
> ---
>
> ## Question 4: If We Say No, Why?
>
> Decline reasons should be documented from an objective list, not invented for each prospect. The following are Meritage's standard decline categories:
>
> | Decline Reason | Description |
> |---|---|
> | Valuation gap | Seller's price expectations are materially above what the market will support, and the Seller is unwilling to engage with market data |
> | Financial quality | Financial statements are insufficient, non-auditable, or require normalization that cannot be completed before going to market |
> | Owner dependence — no succession | The business cannot operate without the owner and no succession plan is in place or feasible |
> | Customer concentration — unmitigated | A single customer represents more than 50% of revenue with no contractual protection or mitigation plan |
> | Seller not motivated | The owner is exploring the market without genuine intent to transact within a reasonable timeline |
> | Timeline pressure | The Seller requires a closing timeline so short that it structurally benefits the buyer and creates unacceptable risk for the Seller |
> | Undisclosed material issues | Legal, regulatory, environmental, or financial issues were not disclosed upfront and materially affect the qualification assessment |
> | Advisor relationship concern | Cultural or interpersonal dynamics suggest the engagement relationship will be unproductive or adversarial |
> | Market conditions | Current market conditions make it unlikely that a qualified buyer will transact at a price acceptable to the Seller |
> | Out of scope | The business type, size, or industry is outside Meritage's current advisory scope |
>
> When a prospect is declined, Meritage communicates the specific reason clearly and, where appropriate, provides a referral to a more appropriate resource. A respectful decline preserves the relationship and the possibility of a future engagement.

> ## Standards
>
> **What excellent looks like in this module:**
>
> An excellent qualification assessment is completed within 10 business days of the initial discovery conversation. It includes a documented 5P Assessment with written notes on each lens, a completed Qualification Scorecard, a clear decision with rationale, and — if the decision is conditional or a decline — a written communication to the prospect.
>
> The Principal Advisor can articulate, in plain language, why they made the qualification decision they made. The assessment reflects the business as it actually is — not as the Seller describes it or as the advisor wishes it were. Every material concern identified during qualification is documented, regardless of the decision.
>
> **Minimum acceptable quality:**
>
> - Discovery conversation completed and documented before a qualification decision is made
> - - 5P Assessment completed with at least one written note per lens
>   - - Qualification Score completed with category-level breakdown
>     - - Decision communicated to the prospect within 5 business days of the assessment
>      
>       - **Red flags indicating the qualification stage was not completed properly:**
>      
>       - - An engagement was accepted without completing the discovery conversation
>         - - The Qualification Score was completed after the engagement decision rather than before
>           - - Material concerns identified during qualification were not documented
>             - - A decline was not communicated — the prospect simply stopped hearing from Meritage

> ## Common Failure Modes
>
> **1. Taking the engagement because the retainer is appealing.**
> Fee revenue is not a qualification criterion. An engagement that generates a retainer but fails to close damages Meritage's reputation more than passing on the engagement would have. The qualification decision must be made independently of fee considerations.
>
> **2. Believing the Seller's narrative without independent verification.**
> Sellers are optimistic about their businesses. They describe their addbacks as certain, their customer relationships as permanent, their management teams as capable. Qualification requires pressing beyond the narrative to the evidence. Ask to see the financials. Ask who manages the top customer relationship. Ask what would happen if the owner took a 6-month leave of absence.
>
> **3. Treating a conditional engagement as a qualified engagement.**
> "We'll address the financial documentation issue during the engagement" is not a qualification decision — it is an avoidance of one. If the condition is material enough to note, it is material enough to require resolution before the engagement begins.
>
> **4. Not documenting the decline.**
> Every decline that is not documented is institutional knowledge lost. The reasons Meritage says no to prospects are among the most valuable data points the organization possesses. They should be recorded, aggregated, and reviewed.
>
> **5. Being persuaded by persistence.**
> A Seller who pushes back hard on a qualification concern — insisting the concern is overstated, that they have a plan, that other advisors didn't have this concern — is demonstrating exactly the lack of advisor receptivity that made the concern relevant in the first place. Persistence is not a qualification factor. The data is.
>
> **6. Skipping the valuation expectations check.**
> The most common source of failed engagements is a valuation gap that was visible during qualification but not addressed. The qualification conversation must include a direct discussion of where the market is likely to price this business. The Seller's response to that discussion is a qualification data point.

> ## AI Assistance
>
> **AI can assist with:**
>
> - Researching the prospect's industry, market size, and recent M&A activity before the discovery conversation
> - - Researching comparable transactions to prepare a preliminary valuation range for the discovery conversation
>   - - Drafting the 5P Assessment template and populating factual sections from notes provided by the advisor
>     - - Summarizing discovery conversation notes into the standard qualification format
>       - - Identifying industry-specific risks and customer concentration benchmarks for the sector
>         - - Drafting written communications to prospects regarding conditional requirements or declines
>           - - Tracking qualification pipeline and outcomes for pattern analysis
>            
>             - **AI should not:**
>            
>             - - Make the qualification decision — that requires advisor judgment and cannot be delegated
>               - - Evaluate the Seller's motivation or cultural fit — those assessments require human observation
>                 - - Represent any preliminary valuation range as a Meritage valuation
>                   - - Contact prospects or referral partners on Meritage's behalf
