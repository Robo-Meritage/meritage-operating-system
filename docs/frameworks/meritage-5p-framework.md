# The Meritage 5P Framework

> The proprietary lens through which Meritage evaluates every sell-side opportunity. Not a checklist — a structured way of seeing a business clearly before committing to represent it.
>
> **Type:** Framework (mental model)
> **Applies to:** All qualification assessments; referenced throughout the engagement lifecycle
> **Owner:** Principal Advisor
>
> ---
>
> ## What This Is
>
> The Meritage 5P Framework is the evaluation structure Meritage uses to assess whether a business is a qualified candidate for a sell-side engagement. It organizes the qualification conversation into five distinct lenses, each asking a different question about the same opportunity.
>
> The 5Ps are:
>
> 1. **People** — Who owns and operates this business, and will they survive a sale process?
> 2. 2. **Performance** — What has the business actually done financially and operationally?
>    3. 3. **Position** — Where does this business stand in its market, and why does it win?
>       4. 4. **Potential** — What can a buyer do with this business that the current owner cannot or has not?
>          5. 5. **Preparedness** — Is the Seller — and the business — ready to go through a transaction?
>            
>             6. Every opportunity Meritage considers looks different through each lens. A business with exceptional Performance may have weak Preparedness. A business with strong Potential may have thin Position. The 5P Framework ensures that enthusiasm for one lens does not cause blindness to another.
>            
>             7. ---
>            
>             8. ## Why Five Lenses
>
> The purpose of the 5P Framework is not to generate a score or fill a checklist. The purpose is to ensure that the qualification conversation covers the dimensions that actually predict whether an engagement will succeed.
>
> Most failed engagements can be traced back to a weakness in one or more of the 5Ps that was visible during qualification but was not taken seriously enough. The 5P Framework makes those weaknesses explicit before Meritage commits its time and reputation to the engagement.
>
> The five lenses are also teachable. A new advisor can learn to apply the 5P Framework consistently. A Principal Advisor can use it to debrief a referral partner: *"We saw strong People and Performance, but Preparedness is a concern — the Seller hasn't had a financial audit in three years."* The framework creates a shared vocabulary for qualification conversations.
>

>
> ## The Five Lenses
>
> ### P1: People
>
> **The question:** Who owns and operates this business, and what happens to each of them in a sale?
>
> People is the first lens because every other dimension depends on it. A business with strong Performance but an owner who is not genuinely motivated to sell will fail the process regardless of the financial quality. A business with strong Potential but no management team will trade at a significant discount regardless of the growth narrative.
>
> **What People evaluates:**
>
> - **Owner profile:** Is the owner genuinely motivated to sell, or exploring the market? What is driving the decision — retirement, health, family circumstance, financial need, opportunity? Is the timeline realistic?
> - - **Owner readiness:** Has the owner mentally prepared for the transition? Do they understand that the process will be demanding, that the final price is uncertain, and that they will be scrutinized by buyers?
>   - - **Owner dependence:** How much of the business's value depends on the owner's personal relationships, skills, or presence? Can the business operate and generate revenue without the owner on a day-to-day basis?
>     - - **Management team:** Is there a team capable of continuing operations post-close? Are key managers retained, incentivized, and unlikely to leave during the process?
>       - - **Key person risk:** Are there employees — other than the owner — whose departure would materially damage the business? Are retention plans in place?
>         - - **Cultural fit:** Is the owner someone Meritage can work with through a difficult, high-stakes process? Do they take advice? Are they honest about the business's weaknesses?
>          
>           - **What excellent looks like:** The owner is genuinely motivated, has a realistic timeline, understands what the process requires, and has a management team capable of operating the business without them. The owner takes advice and will not let emotions override data.
>          
>           - **Disqualifying signals in People:**
>           - - Owner is exploring the market without genuine intent to sell
>             - - Owner is emotionally attached to a price that the market will not support and will not engage with data
> - Owner dependence is complete — the business cannot function without them and no succession plan exists
> - - Key person risks are unmitigated and the owner is unwilling to address them before going to market
>   - - Relationship with the owner suggests the engagement will be contentious or unproductive
>    
>     - ---
>
> ### P2: Performance
>
> **The question:** What has the business actually done, and how defensible are those results?
>
> Performance is the financial and operational track record of the business. It answers the question every buyer will ask first: *Is this real?* A business that cannot demonstrate consistent, normalized, defensible financial performance will not close at the valuation the owner expects — regardless of how compelling the growth story is.
>
> **What Performance evaluates:**
>
> - **Revenue trend:** Is revenue growing, stable, or declining? What is the three-year trend? Is there a story that explains any departures from trend?
> - - **EBITDA and margin quality:** What is the normalized EBITDA? Is the margin defensible or is it inflated by addbacks that may not survive buyer scrutiny?
>   - - **Revenue quality:** Is revenue recurring or transactional? Are there contracts? Is the customer base diversified or concentrated?
>     - - **Financial statement quality:** Are the financials CPA-prepared? Are they clear and auditable? Is there evidence of commingling or irregularity that will cause a buyer to discount the financials?
>       - - **Addback defensibility:** Are the owner addbacks real, documentable, and reasonable in amount? Or are they aggressive claims that will collapse under quality-of-earnings review?
>         - - **Operational consistency:** Does the business perform consistently, or are results lumpy and hard to predict? Is there seasonality that needs to be explained?
>          
>           - **What excellent looks like:** Three years of growing or stable revenue, normalized EBITDA that is clearly documentable, CPA-prepared financials, recurring revenue with diversified customers, and addbacks that are modest, documented, and defensible.
>          
>           - **Disqualifying signals in Performance:**
>           - - Revenue is declining with no credible explanation or recovery plan
>             - - EBITDA relies on addbacks that cannot be documented or that a buyer's QOE team will reverse
> - Financial statements are cash-basis, prepared by the owner, or have significant irregularities
> - - A single customer represents more than 40% of revenue with no mitigation
>   - - The business has material contingent liabilities (pending litigation, tax exposure) not disclosed upfront
>    
>     - ---
>
> ### P3: Position
>
> **The question:** Where does this business stand in its market, and why does it win?
>
> Position is the business's competitive standing — what makes it defensible, what makes customers choose it, and what would make a buyer value it above the financial fundamentals. A business with strong Performance but weak Position is a commodity. A business with strong Position can command a premium.
>
> **What Position evaluates:**
>
> - **Competitive differentiation:** Why do customers choose this business over alternatives? Is the advantage durable or easily replicated?
> - - **Market standing:** Is this business a leader, a niche player, or a commodity provider in its market? What is its reputation?
>   - - **Customer relationships:** How strong and sticky are customer relationships? Are they personal (to the owner) or institutional (to the business)?
>     - - **Geographic or regulatory barriers:** Does the business benefit from barriers to entry — geographic concentration, licensing requirements, certifications, proprietary relationships?
>       - - **Brand and reputation:** Does the business have a reputation in its market that has value independent of its financial performance?
>         - - **Competitive threats:** What competitive dynamics could erode the business's position in the near term? Is the industry consolidating, and if so, is this business a platform or a target?
>          
>           - **What excellent looks like:** The business has a clear reason why customers choose it, a reputation that will survive due diligence inquiry, and competitive advantages that are not solely dependent on the owner's relationships or personality.
>          
>           - **Disqualifying signals in Position:**
>           - - The business has no identifiable differentiation — it competes solely on price
>             - - The owner's personal relationships are the only source of competitive advantage and those relationships will not transfer
> - The market is contracting or disrupted in ways that make the business's current position unsustainable
> - - Competitive threats are material and not disclosed or understood by the owner
>  
>   - ---
>
> ### P4: Potential
>
> **The question:** What can a buyer do with this business that the current owner cannot or has not?
>
> Potential is the growth narrative — the case for why a buyer will pay more than the historical financials suggest the business is worth. It is the answer to the strategic buyer's question: *what synergies exist?* and the financial buyer's question: *what is the growth path?*
>
> **What Potential evaluates:**
>
> - **Organic growth levers:** Are there identifiable avenues for revenue growth that the current owner has not pursued? Geographic expansion, product adjacency, underserved customer segments, pricing power?
> - - **Platform potential:** Is this business a platform for a roll-up or add-on acquisition strategy? Does it create an entry point into a fragmented market?
>   - - **Operational improvement:** Are there cost efficiencies or operational improvements that a more sophisticated owner could capture?
>     - - **Strategic value to a specific buyer:** Is there a category of buyer for whom this business creates disproportionate strategic value — market share, geographic footprint, technology capability, customer access?
>       - - **Management capacity:** Does the business have the management team to execute on a growth plan, or will a buyer need to invest in leadership first?
>         - - **Capital access:** Would additional capital — from a PE sponsor or a well-capitalized strategic buyer — meaningfully change the growth trajectory?
>          
>           - **What excellent looks like:** There are at least two identifiable growth avenues the current owner has not pursued, the business is in a sector with active M&A interest, and there is a plausible case for why a specific category of buyer would pay above the financial buyer range.
>          
>           - **Signals that Potential is weak:**
>           - - The business is fully optimized with no remaining growth levers
>             - - The market is mature and declining with no platform or strategic value
> - The owner believes maximum value has already been achieved
> - - No buyer category would pay a strategic premium for this specific business
>  
>   - Note: Weak Potential is not a disqualifier in the same way that disqualifying signals in People or Performance are. A business with modest Potential but strong Performance, Position, and Preparedness may still be an excellent engagement for the right buyer at the right multiple.
>  
>   - ---
>
> ### P5: Preparedness
>
> **The question:** Is the Seller — and the business — ready to go through a transaction?
>
> Preparedness is the most underrated lens in the 5P Framework, and the most common source of failed engagements. A business that scores well on People, Performance, Position, and Potential can still fail a sale process if the Seller is not prepared for what the process requires.
>
> **What Preparedness evaluates:**
>
> - **Financial documentation:** Are three years of financials available, organized, and CPA-prepared? Are tax returns filed and current? Is the addback schedule documented?
> - - **Data room readiness:** Does the business have the basic documentation that due diligence will require — customer contracts, vendor agreements, employment agreements, corporate records, IP documentation?
>   - - **Operational documentation:** Are key processes documented? Is there evidence that the business could be explained to a new owner without the current owner's presence?
>     - - **Timeline realism:** Is the Seller's timeline for closing realistic given the complexity of the transaction? Do they understand that a typical LMM sell-side process takes 6-12 months from engagement to close?
>       - - **Expectations calibration:** Does the Seller have realistic expectations about valuation, deal structure, earnouts, and transition requirements? Have those expectations been tested against market data?
>         - - **Advisor team:** Does the Seller have a CPA and attorney engaged, or willing to engage, to support the transaction? Are those advisors transaction-experienced?
>           - - **Pre-market value improvements:** Are there known issues — undocumented addbacks, missing contracts, leadership gaps, deferred capital expenditures — that should be addressed before going to market?
>            
>             - **What excellent looks like:** Financials are organized and CPA-prepared, the Seller has realistic expectations grounded in data, the timeline is flexible enough to do the process correctly, and the Seller has engaged or is ready to engage transaction-experienced legal and accounting counsel.
>            
>             - **Disqualifying signals in Preparedness:**
>             - - The Seller needs to close in 60 days due to a crisis (health, financial, or otherwise) that will put Meritage under time pressure that benefits only the buyer
>               - - Financials are not available, are cash-basis only, or have not been prepared by a CPA
> - The Seller is unwilling to engage outside legal or accounting counsel
> - - The Seller refuses to accept any timeline longer than their stated preference, regardless of market conditions
>

> ## Applying the 5P Framework
>
> The 5P Framework is applied during the qualification stage — specifically during and after the qualification discovery conversation. Each lens is evaluated, and a qualitative assessment (Strong / Adequate / Weak / Disqualifying) is recorded.
>
> The framework does not generate a binary go/no-go decision. It generates a structured picture of the opportunity that the Principal Advisor uses to make a judgment: take the engagement, decline, or take with conditions (pre-market improvements required first).
>
> **Take with conditions** is one of the most important outputs of the 5P evaluation. It allows Meritage to say: *"We want to represent you, but not until X is addressed."* This protects both Meritage and the Seller — it prevents going to market with a business that is not ready, and it gives the Seller a concrete path to becoming a qualified candidate.
>
> The 5P assessment is also the basis for the Qualification Score (see `docs/templates/client-qualification-scorecard.md`) and the Qualification Standard (see `docs/standards/client-qualification-standard.md`).

> ## The 5P Framework in Ongoing Advisory Work
>
> The 5P Framework does not disappear after qualification. It recurs throughout the engagement:
>
> - **Valuation (Module 04):** The Performance and Potential lenses inform the multiple selection and value driver assessment
> - - **Positioning (Module 05):** The Position and Potential lenses are the foundation of the investment thesis
>   - - **GTM (Module 06):** The Potential lens informs the buyer universe and strategic fit analysis
>     - - **Management Calls (Module 09):** The People lens informs how the Seller is coached and presented
>       - - **Due Diligence (Module 11):** The Preparedness lens determines how smooth or difficult diligence will be
>        
>         - The 5P Framework is the common language for the entire engagement. Using it consistently — from qualification through close — creates coherence across stages.
