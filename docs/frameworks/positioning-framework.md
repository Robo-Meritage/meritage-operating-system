# Meritage Positioning Framework

> The reusable mental model for translating a valuation into a market narrative. This framework teaches *how to think about positioning*. For what a complete positioning deliverable must contain, see [docs/standards/](../standards/).
>
> **Type:** Framework (mental model)
> **Applies to:** All sell-side engagements
> **Owner:** Principal Advisor
>
> ---
>
> ## The Core Question
>
> Valuation determines what a business is worth.
>
> Positioning determines *why a specific buyer should pay more for it than another buyer would.*
>
> These are not the same question. A business generating $2M in EBITDA will trade at different multiples depending on who is buying, what they need, and how well the investment narrative speaks to their specific strategic goals. The purpose of positioning is to identify the buyer who will pay the most, understand why, and construct a narrative that makes the business legible and compelling to that buyer.
>
> ---
>
> ## The Positioning Equation
>
> ```
> Investment Value = Financial Performance + Strategic Fit + Growth Optionality − Risk Discount
> ```
>
> This is not a mathematical formula. It is a framework for what drives value above and beyond the EBITDA multiple.
>
> **Financial Performance** is what the business has already demonstrated — normalized EBITDA, revenue trend, margin quality, cash flow consistency. This is the floor. Every buyer evaluates this.
>
> **Strategic Fit** is the value the business creates for a specific buyer that it would not create for others. A business that gives a strategic buyer a new geography, a new customer set, or the ability to eliminate a competitor is worth more to that buyer than its standalone financial performance suggests. A business that gives a financial buyer a quality platform in a fragmented market is worth more than one that offers no roll-up potential.
>
> **Growth Optionality** is the believable case for value creation after close. Not aspirational projections — specific, supportable avenues: geographic expansion into markets where demand exists, product adjacency that customers have already requested, operational leverage from technology adoption, or talent that has been retained but underutilized.
>
> **Risk Discount** is the reduction in value that buyers apply when they see unresolved risks: owner dependency, customer concentration, deferred CapEx, financial statement quality concerns. Positioning does not eliminate these risks. It acknowledges them honestly and provides a mitigation narrative that prevents the buyer from applying a larger discount than the risk actually warrants.
>

> ## The Buyer Lens Framework
>
> Every business looks different through a different buyer's lens. Positioning means choosing the lens before going to market.
>
> ### Financial Buyer Lens
>
> A financial buyer (private equity, family office, independent sponsor) asks:
> - Is the EBITDA real, defensible, and recurring?
> - - Is the management team capable of operating the business without the owner?
>   - - What is the growth path? Can I put this business on a platform and grow it?
>     - - What is the exit story? In 5-7 years, who will buy this from me and why?
>       - - How much of the purchase price can I finance, and what is my return on equity?
>        
>         - For a financial buyer, the investment narrative must lead with financial quality, management team strength, and platform potential. The growth story must be believable without the owner.
>        
>         - ### Strategic Buyer Lens
>        
>         - A strategic buyer asks:
> - What does this give me that I cannot build or buy more cheaply?
> - - How does this accelerate my existing strategy?
>   - - What synergies can I capture — cost, revenue, capability, market access?
>     - - What integration risk am I taking on?
>       - - What is my valuation rationale to my own board or investors?
>        
>         - For a strategic buyer, the investment narrative must lead with the strategic fit — the specific reason this acquisition is worth more to this buyer than the standalone financial model suggests. The narrative should speak the buyer's language: market share, geographic footprint, product suite, customer relationships, technology capability.
>        
>         - ### Owner-Operator Lens
>        
>         - An owner-operator (individual buyer, search fund) asks:
> - Can I run this business?
> - - Is the cash flow sufficient to service acquisition financing and pay me a living?
>   - - What does the transition period look like?
>     - - What is the downside if this doesn't work?
>      
>       - For an owner-operator, the narrative must demonstrate operational transferability and cash flow reliability. Complexity and owner dependency are concerns, not features.
>      
>       - ### ESOP Lens
>      
>       - An ESOP evaluates:
> - Does the business have sufficient cash flow to service ESOP financing?
> - - Is the management team capable of running the business independently?
>   - - Is the seller willing to accept a structure that prioritizes employee ownership over maximum proceeds?
>    
>     - For an ESOP, the narrative must demonstrate financial stability, management depth, and cultural alignment with the ESOP structure.
>

> ## The Investment Thesis Construction Test
>
> A well-constructed investment thesis passes four tests:
>
> **The Specificity Test**
> Every sentence in the investment thesis must apply to this company specifically — not to its industry, not to its category, not to its geography. If a sentence could describe the average business in this sector, it is not positioning — it is description.
>
> *Generic:* "Leading provider of HVAC services in a fragmented market."
> *Specific:* "The only HVAC contractor in the Greater Columbus market with both commercial and residential capabilities, a 12-year relationship with the region's largest property management company, and an existing technician workforce that would take a new entrant 18 months to replicate."
>
> **The Buyer Test**
> The investment thesis should be written for a specific buyer type. Before writing it, ask: who is most likely to pay the target price for this business, and what do they need to hear? The thesis should speak directly to that buyer's decision-making criteria.
>
> **The Defensibility Test**
> Every claim in the investment thesis must survive diligence. Before including a claim, ask: can we document this? Will it still be true when the buyer looks closely? If the claim is aspirational, label it as such and ground it in evidence.
>
> **The Goldman Sachs Test**
> Read the investment thesis and ask: could this have been written by Goldman Sachs for a Fortune 500 company? Could it have been written by any regional broker for any business in this industry? If yes, it is not specific enough. The investment thesis should be recognizably about this business and this opportunity.

> ## The Risk Framing Principle
>
> Positioning does not mean hiding risks. It means framing them.
>
> Every material risk should be identified, contextualized, and given a mitigation position before the process begins. The failure mode is not acknowledging a risk — it is letting a buyer discover it without context and apply their own interpretation.
>
> **Unframed risk:** "The owner handles all customer relationships."
>
> **Framed risk:** "The owner has been the primary customer relationship holder. However, three of the five largest customers have established relationships with the senior operations team over the past 18 months, and the owner has committed to a 12-month transition period. A retention plan for the operations lead is in place as a condition of close."
>
> The framed version acknowledges the same risk. It does not hide it. But it provides context that prevents the buyer from treating it as a disqualifying factor.

> ## The Positioning-Valuation Relationship
>
> Positioning does not change the valuation analysis. It changes the conversation about value.
>
> The valuation produces a range. Positioning is the argument for why this business belongs at the top of that range for the right buyer. The positioning work does not override the financial analysis — it contextualizes it.
>
> When the valuation analysis produces a range that is below the Seller's expectations, positioning cannot close the gap. That gap is a qualification and expectation management problem, not a positioning problem. Positioning can only justify a premium for a business that has earned one.
>
> Conversely, when a business has genuine strategic value that is not fully captured in its standalone financial performance, positioning is the mechanism for surfacing that value and ensuring the right buyers understand it. Some of the most important work Meritage does is identifying strategic buyers who will pay above the financial buyer range — and constructing the narrative that explains why the premium is justified.
>

> ## Positioning in Practice: The Three Narratives
>
> Every engagement eventually produces three versions of the positioning narrative, each calibrated for a different audience:
>
> **The Internal Narrative** (Positioning Brief)
> The full strategic analysis: buyer personas, investment thesis, risk register, synergy hypotheses, growth narrative. For Meritage's internal use. Informs all external communications but is not shared with buyers.
>
> **The Teaser Narrative**
> A two-page summary designed to generate interest without disclosing the Company's identity. Leads with the investment thesis. Does not include financial details sufficient to identify the Company. Designed to separate serious buyers from browsers.
>
> **The CIM Narrative**
> The full investment narrative: investment highlights, business overview, financial performance, growth strategy, management team, and the investment thesis in full. Written for buyers who have executed NDAs. The CIM executes the positioning strategy — it is not where positioning is developed.
>
> All three narratives draw from the same Positioning Brief. The difference is depth and disclosure, not substance. If the narratives are inconsistent, the Positioning Brief is incomplete.
