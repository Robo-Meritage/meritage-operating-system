> >>

> >> ## Decision-Making Philosophy
> >>
> >> **Principles over rules** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel consistently rejects rigid rule sets in favor of principles that can be applied with judgment. The MOS itself reflects this: modules describe thinking and standards, not scripts. When an advisor asks "what do I do if...", the answer is usually a framework for thinking, not a predetermined answer.
> >>
> >> **Tradeoffs, not recommendations** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel expects recommendations to include tradeoffs, not just conclusions. A recommendation that says "do X" without acknowledging what X costs or what you give up by not doing Y is incomplete. This applies to everything: engagement decisions, fee structures, negotiation posture, document architecture.
> >>
> >> **Systems over heroics** *[Confirmed]*
> >> The MOS itself is evidence of this. Daniel builds systems that produce consistent results rather than relying on individual brilliance for each situation. The goal is always: *Could someone else do this at Meritage's standard if I weren't here?*
> >>
> >> **Long-term relationships over short-term optimization** *[Confirmed]*
> >> In negotiations and client relationships, Daniel consistently prioritizes the long-term relationship over extracting maximum value from any single transaction. A deal structure that works for both parties and produces a positive relationship is worth more than a marginally better outcome that creates resentment. This applies to fees, terms, and the posture of difficult conversations.
> >>
> >> **Operational clarity as a value** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel has a strong preference for documents, processes, and communications that are clear to someone reading them without context. Ambiguity is treated as a failure mode, not an acceptable outcome.
> >>
> >> **Framework thinking** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel consistently thinks in frameworks: the 5P Framework, the IP Pyramid, the Capability Maturity Model. When faced with a new problem, the instinct is to build a mental model first, then apply it consistently. Ad hoc decisions are a last resort.
> >>
> >> **Scale-oriented design** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel builds businesses, processes, and documents with the expectation they will need to scale. This means resisting the temptation to solve for the current situation at the expense of the repeatable pattern.

> >> ## Writing and Communication Preferences
> >>
> >> **Directness over diplomacy** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel prefers direct language. "We decline this engagement" is better than "we may want to consider whether this engagement aligns with our current priorities." Diplomatic hedging in professional documents is treated as a quality failure.
> >>
> >> **No legalese unless legally necessary** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel actively rejects unnecessary legalese in business documents. If a sentence reads like it was written by a lawyer to protect against hypothetical liability rather than to communicate a real idea, it should be rewritten. Legal precision belongs in legal documents. Operating documents should read like they were written by someone who thinks clearly.
> >>
> >> **The Goldman Sachs Test** *[Confirmed]*
> >> If a sentence could apply equally well to Goldman Sachs, a regional business broker, and Meritage, it is not specific enough. Generic consulting language is treated as a quality failure. Every document should describe *how Meritage specifically operates*, not how a hypothetical advisory firm operates.
> >>
> >> **Short sentences over long ones** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel's own writing tends toward short, direct sentences. Documents written in his voice should do the same. Long compound sentences that bury the point are a writing quality issue.
> >>
> >> **Structure before prose** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel thinks structurally. He will often propose an outline before filling in content. When asked for a document, a good first step is to propose the structure and get alignment before writing the full text.
> >>
> >> **Plain language for client-facing documents** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Client-facing documents (engagement letters, fee explanations, onboarding summaries) should be written so that a successful business owner with no M&A background can read them without confusion. This is not dumbing down — it is respecting the reader's time.

> >> ## Advisory Philosophy
> >>
> >> **Advisor-centric, not broker-centric** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Meritage's value is in judgment, not transaction facilitation. A broker moves paper. An advisor changes outcomes. Daniel consistently positions Meritage as the latter and builds processes that reflect it. Qualification is advisor-led. Engagement terms are advisor-set. GTM launch timing is advisor-determined.
> >>
> >> **Candor as a service** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel treats honesty about difficult realities — valuation gaps, unrealistic expectations, weak financials — as a core advisory service, not a risk to the relationship. Advisors who defer hard conversations to protect short-term comfort are a failure mode. Clients deserve the truth early enough to do something about it.
> >>
> >> **Preparation is non-negotiable** *[Confirmed]*
> >> The management call preparation requirement (ODR-004) is one example of a broader pattern: Daniel does not accept "we'll figure it out on the call" as a working posture. Preparation is the difference between a controlled process and a reactive one.
> >>
> >> **Reputation as a compounding asset** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel treats Meritage's reputation with the buyer community as a long-term compounding asset. A firm that brings well-prepared, realistic sellers to market earns access to better buyers. A firm that brings unprepared sellers or inflated expectations loses it. Every engagement decision is implicitly a reputation decision.
> >>
> >> **Decline is a quality signal** *[Pending: Daniel to confirm or expand]*
> >> The hypothesis is that Meritage's willingness to formally decline engagements that don't meet the qualification standard is treated internally as a sign of quality, not a failure. A high close rate on completed engagements is more valuable than a high engagement volume. Confirm whether this is an active belief or an aspirational one.

> >> ## What Works Well in AI Collaboration
> >>
> >> **Present the structure first** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel responds better to a proposed structure (outline, framework, plan) before content is filled in. This lets him redirect before significant work is done in the wrong direction.
> >>
> >> **Don't ask unnecessary clarifying questions** *[Confirmed]*
> >> If the intent is reasonably clear, proceed and present the result. Excessive clarifying questions slow the work and signal lack of confidence. The exception: if proceeding in the wrong direction would require significant rework, a single clarifying question is appropriate.
> >>
> >> **Flag uncertainty explicitly** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel appreciates when an AI system says "I'm uncertain about this — here's what I know and here's what I'd need to know to be more confident" rather than presenting uncertain information with false confidence. Mark uncertain entries as *Pending* rather than guessing.
> >>
> >> **Tradeoffs, always** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Any recommendation should acknowledge what it costs. "Here's what I recommend, and here's what you give up by doing it this way" is the expected format.
> >>
> >> **Show your reasoning** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel is more likely to accept a recommendation when the reasoning is transparent. Don't just state conclusions. Show the logic chain.
> >>
> >> **Be direct about quality** *[Confirmed]*
> >> If a document, argument, or decision has a quality problem, say so directly. Don't soften the assessment to avoid discomfort. Daniel treats direct professional feedback as a sign of respect, not aggression.

> >> ## What Doesn't Work
> >>
> >> **Generic language** *[Confirmed]*
> >> See the Goldman Sachs Test above. Generic consulting language that could apply to any firm is the single most reliable way to produce a document Daniel won't use.
> >>
> >> **Rigid prescriptions without judgment** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Documents that say "always do X" without acknowledging context are poorly calibrated to how Daniel actually operates. Standards and processes should describe principles and thresholds, then leave room for advisor judgment.
> >>
> >> **Unnecessary padding** *[Confirmed]*
> >> Daniel prefers concise, dense documents over long ones padded with qualifications and caveats. Every sentence should earn its place.
> >>
> >> **Telling Daniel what he already knows** *[Confirmed]*
> >> When Daniel states a position or makes a decision, he doesn't need it summarized back to him before the work begins. Acknowledge it and move forward.
