## Referral Partner Agreement Review — 9 Issues to Watch

When reviewing any referral or affiliate partner agreement, the following issues appear in standard boilerplate and should be checked:

### 1. Definition of "Qualifying Transaction" (HIGHEST RISK)
Standard boilerplate often defines qualifying transactions extremely broadly to include: mergers, recaps, minority investments, financings, partnerships, JVs, equity raises, asset sales, restructurings.

**The risk:** If they introduce a company today, and 2.5 years later Meritage helps that company raise minority capital, the referral partner may claim a fee. This is much broader than a simple M&A referral arrangement.

**Correction:** Limit to "completed M&A transaction engagements directly resulting from the introduced target."

### 2. Tail Period Length
Standard boilerplate: 3 years from introduction date.

**Meritage standard:** 12 months preferred (18 months maximum for lightweight referral relationships).

### 3. EBITDA Threshold Not in Agreement
The email negotiation established "$3M EBITDA minimum" but the Order Form may not include this threshold, allowing any size company to count.

**Correction:** Insert explicit language: "Targets shall consist of businesses with trailing twelve month EBITDA of not less than $3,000,000."

### 4. No "Procuring Cause" Requirement
Standard boilerplate: the referral partner only needs to "introduce" the target. This allows them to claim a fee even if Meritage independently built the relationship later.

**Correction:** Require "first introduced by Consultant and materially involved in originating the relationship resulting in the Qualifying Transaction."

### 5. No Definition of "Introduction"
What counts? Email blast? LinkedIn message? Mutual CRM overlap? This becomes operationally messy.

**Correction:** Require a documented introduction acknowledged by Meritage in writing.

### 6. Payment Calculation is Vague
Standard: "percentage of aggregate commission, fee, or other form of remuneration."

**Correction:** "10% of fees actually received by Client directly attributable to a Qualifying Transaction involving a Target introduced by Consultant."

### 7. Trial Framework Missing from Agreement
The trial structure (5 introductions, evaluate fit, no expansion without mutual agreement) should be explicitly in the agreement or its Exhibit, not just in email negotiations.

**Correction:** Add an Initial Trial Period section: "Consultant shall provide up to five (5) introductions... Following completion of the initial five introductions, any continuation of the relationship shall require mutual written agreement."

### 8. Real Estate Licensing Language
Look for language like "Client is a licensed real estate broker" or references to real estate licensing requirements. This is recycled boilerplate. For Meritage (M&A advisor, not real estate broker), this should be removed entirely.

### 9. Most Favored Nation Clause
If present, forces Meritage to give this partner better economics if Meritage ever offers another referral partner better terms. This limits future dealmaking and should be removed.

## Why

The DealJet conversation illustrates a recurring dynamic in partner/affiliate agreements:

**"Standard counsel-generated platform boilerplate is designed for broad protection, not tailored to your actual negotiated arrangement."**

The email negotiation may be simple and fair. The agreement that follows often is not. This discrepancy is usually not bad intent — it's the result of sending a standard template without customizing it to the specific deal.

Meritage's response: send a concise, professional cleanup email with requested revisions. Not to kill the deal, but to protect operational clarity — especially for scenarios where the same company is touched by multiple sourcing channels over multiple years.
