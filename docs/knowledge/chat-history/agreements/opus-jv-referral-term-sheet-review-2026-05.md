## Learned

### The "Separate JV from Referral" Pattern

When a prospective partner conflates two different business relationship types, Meritage's pattern is to separate them explicitly:

**Independent Sponsor JV** = Both parties contribute materially to sourcing, capital relationships, pitching, and execution. True partnership with shared economics on net revenue.

**CEO/Business-Owner Referral** = One party introduces clients to Meritage. Meritage executes. Referral party receives a percentage of Meritage's fee.

**Why separate:** The economics, accountability, and legal protections are fundamentally different. Conflating them in one agreement creates ambiguity about who owns what, leads to fee disputes, and restricts normal business development.

## JV Issues Still Requiring Negotiation

### 1. "Net Revenue" Definition
Needs precise definition of what expenses are deductible:
- Who approves expenses?
- Can Opus incur expenses on behalf of the JV?
- Is travel reimbursable? Conferences? Analyst costs?

### 2. Minimum Success Fee ($180,000)
Needs clarification: Is this a minimum engagement fee, a minimum success fee, or merely a target? What happens when a deal is too small to support $180K?

### 3. Right of First Look — Process
What happens if Meritage doesn't respond within 14 days? Declines? Can't staff it? Needs a clean release mechanism.

### 4. Co-Pitching Governance
Must define: Who controls engagement letters? Who controls pricing? Who owns the client relationship after a deal? These become contentious later.

### 5. Long-Term Relationship Ownership (BIGGEST OPEN ISSUE)
The JV paper does not answer: who owns a client relationship 5+ years after introduction? If Opus introduces an IS, Meritage executes multiple acquisitions, raises debt, and later handles an exit — who has rights to subsequent work?

This is the most important gap in the JV document and will become the central negotiation point.

## CEO Referral Term Sheet — 6 Issues to Reject or Redline

Lou's referral term sheet contained 6 provisions materially broader than the negotiation implied:

### 1. 40% Gross Revenue — Reject
Lou proposed 40% of gross revenue with no cap. This is not a referral fee — it is a profit share. Standard range for CEO referral: 10–20% of fees on referred client, not gross revenue.

### 2. Five-Year Tail — Reject / Reduce
Five years from first covered-party status or last Opus touchpoint is excessive for a referral relationship. Meritage standard: 12–24 months.

### 3. "Covered Party" Definition Too Broad — Redline
Could capture companies Meritage meets at events simply because both parties were present, as long as Meritage follows up within 90 days. This is an attribution landmine.

### 4. Non-Circumvention Language — Redline
Would prohibit Meritage from engaging any Opus member, attendee, or Opus-introduced party outside the agreement. This restricts normal business development.

### 5. Regulatory Indemnity — Reject
One-sided, uncapped regulatory indemnity with Opus-selected counsel. Requires attorney review before Meritage can entertain.

### 6. Most Favored Nation (MFN) Clause — Remove
Would force Meritage to give Opus better economics if Meritage ever offers another partner better terms. Limits future dealmaking.

## Recommended Response to Lou

"We are much closer on the Independent Sponsor JV than the CEO referral structure. Let's focus on the JV first and get alignment there. Once we establish how we want to handle attribution, economics, and relationship ownership in the IS channel, we can determine whether a broader CEO referral relationship makes sense."

**Why:** Keeps conversation centered on the area where Lou already indicated strong interest. Avoids getting bogged down in the more contentious CEO referral framework.

## Why

The Opus/Lou conversation illustrates that prospective partners often send standard boilerplate designed for maximum protection, not the specific negotiated arrangement. Meritage's response pattern is the same as with DealJet: professional, collaborative redline — not rejection.

The deeper strategic principle: **the gap between the email negotiation and the formal agreement is where future disputes are born**. Tighten the agreement to match what was actually discussed.

## Patterns

- When a partner conflates JV and referral, Meritage separates them.
- Meritage does not accept 40%+ referral fees; typical referral rates are 10–20% of Meritage's fee.
- 12–24 month tail is Meritage's standard for referral relationships.
- The long-term relationship ownership question (who owns a client introduced 5 years ago) must be addressed in any JV document.
- "Standard boilerplate is designed for broad protection, not tailored to the actual negotiated arrangement" — treat it accordingly.
