## Context

Daniel reviewed a call transcript involving four participants (James, Mike, and others) discussing a potential opportunity to replace Watsco as the North American master distributor for Gree HVAC equipment. The group was at an early, speculative stage. This conversation produced Daniel's tiered framework for what must be prepared before Meritage introduces any deal to a capital source (Brian or family offices).

## Framework: Three-Tier Deal Readiness Model

Before Meritage introduces any deal to Brian or institutional capital, Daniel applies a three-tier readiness test. Each tier represents a gate. A deal cannot advance until the prior tier is satisfied.

### Tier 1: Absolutely Required (Gate to Brian Introduction)

Without these items, Daniel will not introduce the opportunity to any capital source.

1. **Opportunity Memo (5–10 pages)** — Professional narrative answering: What happened? Why now? Who are the parties? What is the proposed solution? This is the executive summary for someone with no prior context.
2. **Revenue Validation** — The stated revenue number must be documentable. Required: annual revenue, 3-year history, revenue by product category, revenue by distributor type, revenue concentration, and top 20 distributors. Conversational revenue estimates are not investable.
3. **Gross Margin Analysis** — Full P&L context required: revenue, COGS, gross margin, warranty reserve, freight costs, inventory carry costs, EBITDA. A 30% gross margin business can be wildly profitable, marginally profitable, or losing money depending on overhead. Gross margin alone is insufficient.
4. **Capital Requirement Breakdown** — Any capital ask must be a line-item build (inventory, warehouse, payroll, ERP, insurance, working capital, legal, import costs, warranty reserve). An estimate without itemization is meaningless to investors.
5. **Manufacturer/Counterparty Relationship Assessment** — The single most important issue in any distribution or channel opportunity. Must answer: Has the manufacturer expressed interest? Who makes the decision? Is exclusivity available? Would they support a new platform? No investor funds a distribution thesis before knowing the manufacturer relationship is real.

### Tier 2: Investor-Level Due Diligence

6. **Market Mapping** — Complete ecosystem map: all major distributors, channel partners, key contractors, and competitors. Investors ask why this opportunity rather than alternatives.
7. **Customer Retention Analysis** — Any thesis based on customer migration must be proven, not assumed. Requires: top distributor list, revenue by distributor, estimated retention percentage, and supporting evidence.
8. **Key Personnel List** — Investors fund people first. Requires named individuals with roles and resumes.
9. **Organizational Structure** — Must answer: Who runs the company? Who owns it? Who controls strategy vs. operations?

### Tier 3: Pre-Family Office Introduction

10. **Preliminary Financial Model** — Years 1–5 projections including revenue, gross margin, operating expenses, EBITDA, working capital, and inventory requirements.
11. **Distribution/Operations Footprint Plan** — Current state (warehouse locations, inventory, shipping lanes) and proposed future state (locations, square footage, inventory turns).
12. **Legal Risk Assessment** — Existing agreements, expiration dates, non-competes, non-solicits, exclusivity provisions, IP restrictions. Daniel's note: this is where deals die.

## Critical Distinction: Transfer vs. Startup

A deal that presents itself as a 'transfer of revenue' will be evaluated by investors as a startup until proven otherwise. The burden of proof is on the proposing party to demonstrate:
- Revenue transfers (with documentation)
- Customers transfer (with evidence)
- Distribution transfers (with commitments)
- Manufacturer relationship transfers (with expressed intent)
- Key personnel transfer (with signed commitments)

Until these are documented, the opportunity is an industry thesis, not an actionable transaction.

## What Brian Typically Asks First

Daniel's expectation of Brian's first questions on any deal:
1. Is the counterparty/manufacturer actually interested?
2. What is the EBITDA?
3. What is the equity structure?
4. What is the downside risk?
5. Who is running it?
6. How much capital is required, and how fast can it be deployed?
7. Is there exclusivity?
8. How defensible is the channel/position?
9. Why hasn't someone else already done this?

## Call Management Rule

If a second call is needed with a prospective deal partner still at the 'interesting concept' stage, the second call should NOT repeat the conceptual discussion. It should be structured entirely around collecting the Tier 1 items. Daniel's rule: if the group cannot produce the six core items (financials, footprint, capital requirements, counterparty status, ownership structure, key personnel), the opportunity remains a thesis.
