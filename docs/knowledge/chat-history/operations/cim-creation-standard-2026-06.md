## Context

Daniel compared two CIM versions for Project Alchemy (ClaimWizard): a presentation-style visual CIM and a traditional narrative CIM. He graded them on multiple buyer-facing dimensions, then used the comparison to rewrite the Meritage CIM creation skill from scratch. This record captures the definitive Meritage CIM standard as established in June 2026.

## CIM Format Decision

**Presentation-style CIM wins over narrative CIM for buyer marketing.**

| Category | Presentation CIM | Narrative CIM |
|---|---|---|
| Executive Appeal | A | B+ |
| Readability | A | B |
| Institutional Buyer Appeal | A- | B+ |
| Information Density | B+ | A |
| Professional Appearance | A | B+ |
| Speed to Investment Thesis | A | B |
| Overall Grade | A (8.9/10) | B+ (7.8/10) |

**Why:** PE associates, corp dev teams, and strategic buyers review dozens of opportunities per month. The presentation format allows faster review, easier internal forwarding, and quicker investment committee discussion. Dense narrative reports get skimmed — important information gets buried.

**The Meritage Answer:** A hybrid format. Presentation-style structure with concise buyer-first narrative. Not a pure deck. Not a dense report. The buyer should understand the opportunity within the first 5–7 pages, then find enough supporting detail to justify continued diligence.

## Meritage Standard CIM Structure

The definitive 14-section Meritage CIM outline (as of June 2026):

1. Confidentiality & Disclaimer
2. Executive Summary
3. Company at a Glance
4. Investment Highlights
5. Business Overview
6. Products / Services / Operations
7. Customers, Revenue Mix, and Go-to-Market
8. Market & Industry Overview
9. Management & Employees
10. Growth Opportunities
11. Financial Overview
12. Transaction Overview
13. Appendix / Supporting Exhibits
14. Internal Diligence Checklist (if unresolved items remain — internal only, not buyer-facing)

## Writing Standard

**Buyer-first writing rule:** Start each section with the main takeaway. Support it with facts, metrics, and concise narrative.

**Bad:** 'The Company is a best-in-class provider with significant growth potential.'

**Better:** 'The Company has grown revenue from $X to $Y since 20XX, supported by repeat customer demand, a tenured management team, and expansion into [specific market/channel/service line].'

Rules:
- Use tables, callout boxes, chart prompts, and bullets where they improve readability
- Avoid long blocks of uninterrupted prose
- Avoid unsupported superlatives ('industry-leading', 'best-in-class', 'category leader') unless backed by win rate data, market share, or customer quotes
- Avoid generic investment banking language that could apply to any company
- Use third-person banker tone: professional, direct, supportable
- No [VERIFY] notes in buyer-facing output — move unresolved items to Internal Diligence Checklist
- Maintain consistent company name, entity structure, and deal terminology throughout

## CIM Intake Process

**Setup questions (ask only what is needed — pull from connected sources first):**
1. What company or project name is this CIM for?
2. Is this a full company sale, majority recap, minority recap, capital raise, or other transaction?
3. Who is the expected buyer audience: strategic buyers, PE, family offices, independent sponsors, or mixed?
4. Should the CIM be buyer-ready, internal draft, or outline-only?
5. Do you want presentation-style, narrative, or Meritage hybrid tone?
6. What source folder, deal room, or Google Drive location should I pull from?
7. Are there materials to prioritize (financials, QoE, management presentation, transcript, customer data, org chart, prior CIM)?
8. Are there any sensitivities, exclusions, or items that should NOT appear in the buyer-facing CIM?

**Source gathering priority (pull first, ask second):**
1. Existing CIM, teaser, management presentation, or board deck
2. Financial statements and monthly financials
3. QoE report or EBITDA adjustment support
4. Management call transcripts or notes
5. Customer and revenue data
6. Org chart, employee list, bios, and facility details
7. Market research, industry reports, buyer universe notes
8. Deal process notes, valuation expectations, structure preferences, timeline

## Business Type Classification

Before drafting, classify the business type and adapt KPIs and buyer concerns accordingly. Do not apply SaaS metrics to non-SaaS businesses.

| Business Type | Key Metrics to Emphasize |
|---|---|
| Software / SaaS | ARR, MRR, GRR, NRR, churn, CAC, LTV, ARPU, customer count, product roadmap |
| Home services / trades | Geography, service mix, recurring revenue, lead sources, technician count, fleet, branch performance |
| Manufacturing | Revenue by product, gross margin, capacity utilization, backlog, facilities, equipment, capex |
| Distribution | Revenue by category, supplier/customer concentration, inventory turns, gross margin, logistics |
| Professional services | Backlog, utilization, billable headcount, licenses, repeat clients, project mix, owner dependency |
| Healthcare services | Provider base, locations, payer mix, referral sources, patient volume, licenses, compliance |
| Construction / contracting | Backlog, bonding capacity, project type, margin by job type, safety, crews, equipment |
| Transportation / logistics | Fleet, lanes, driver base, utilization, maintenance, customer contracts, fuel exposure, insurance |
| Industrial services | Recurring service revenue, technicians, certifications, safety, equipment, customer concentration |
| Consumer products | Brand, SKU mix, channel mix, velocity, margin, retail relationships |
| Multi-location services | Location count, same-location performance, unit economics, expansion pipeline |

## Executive Summary Standard

The executive summary must allow a buyer to quickly understand:
- What the company does
- Why the opportunity matters
- Size and profitability
- Revenue quality
- Customer base and market position
- Growth opportunity
- Transaction rationale

Include 4–6 investment highlights. Each highlight must be specific, defensible, and fact-supported.

Include a snapshot financial table adapted to the business type: Revenue, Gross Profit, Adj. EBITDA, Adj. EBITDA Margin, plus sector-specific KPIs.

## Financial Section Standard

Each EBITDA adjustment must include: name, amount, period, rationale, source, and support status.

Internally flag any add-back that is unusually large, recurring, weakly supported, or likely to be challenged by buyers. Do not put these flags in buyer-facing output.

Standard financial exhibits: historical income statement summary, adjusted EBITDA bridge, revenue growth, gross margin, EBITDA margin, working capital, capex, revenue mix, customer concentration, and projections with assumptions.

## Quality Checklist

Before finalizing any buyer-facing CIM:
- [ ] First 5–7 pages clearly explain the opportunity
- [ ] Hybrid presentation/narrative format — not a dense report
- [ ] Business type correctly identified; KPIs and buyer concerns adapted accordingly
- [ ] All financial figures internally consistent across sections
- [ ] EBITDA add-backs described, sourced, and internally flagged if weak
- [ ] No visible [VERIFY] notes in buyer-facing output
- [ ] Unresolved questions moved to internal diligence checklist
- [ ] Investment highlights specific, quantified, and supportable
- [ ] Customer names and sensitive data handled appropriately
- [ ] Market claims sourced where possible
- [ ] Confidentiality notice included
- [ ] Tone professional, third-person, Meritage-aligned
- [ ] 'So what?' test passes for every paragraph
